# Assessment A — Accept the $24M acquisition offer, or stay independent?

## Recommendation
Do not accept the offer as structured, and do not decline blindly. Convert it into a 3–4 week diligence-and-negotiation sprint whose output is a decision, not a deal. The single question that decides this — before any multiple analysis — is why this acquirer wants you now. Confidence: HIGH that accepting as-is would be a mistake; MEDIUM on the ultimate sell/stay call, because it hinges on three facts not yet known.

## Key considerations
The three factors that actually swing the decision (each stated as what would flip the recommendation):
- **Acquirer's real motive.** If this is a defensive block, roadmap gap, acqui-hire, or customer/data grab, 6× is an opening bid below strategic value → push hard or walk. If they are pricing in something they see and you don't (churn, a coming competitor, category consolidation — why now?), the offer may be generous → lean accept. You cannot tell which without diligencing their roadmap, account overlap, and build-vs-buy pressure. This factor alone can invert the answer.
- **The real deal, not the headline.** "All-cash 6×" is not $24M certain. Middle-market deals routinely place 20–30% in escrow/earnout/retention, and earnouts realize only ~50–70% of maximum. Demand a sources-and-uses schedule and a cap-table waterfall at $24M before trusting any per-founder figure — liquidation preferences, unvested equity, and option treatment can break the "55% → $13M" assumption. If ≥20–25% is contingent/retention, the effective price is well under 6×.
- **Founder objective.** "Should we accept" presupposes exit is the goal. Whose goal — founders', company's, or investors'? Interests diverge: after Series-A preference, employees with options may net little; investors may want protection or a swing; founders get liquidity, possibly with handcuffs. The right answer differs by seat.

Also material:
- Valuation vs. alternatives and downside base rates: 6× looks light for 80% growth only if that growth is efficient and defensible; recent Series-A→B graduation is ~9–25% within two years, so the comparison is the probability-weighted, dilution- and time-adjusted later exit, not "6× vs. a market multiple today."
- Reversibility cuts both ways: selling is near-irreversible, but a bad Series B (down-round, heavy preferences, lost board control) is also a one-way door that can destroy founder optionality more severely.
- Second-order effects: an active sale process can chill the raise and unsettle the team; sequence any market check quietly.
- Timing: the Series-B bar has risen (NRR, CAC payback, burn multiple); 80% growth alone won't clear it — test fundability in parallel.
- Founder psychology: the offer's salience anchors the framing; separate company expected-value from founder personal utility and name any fatigue/liquidity motive rather than dressing it as "the company should sell."
- Tax: QSBS and ordinary-income treatment of retention/earnout can swing founder net materially.

## Confidence & what would change the answer
Push hard/walk if diligence shows a strategic/defensive motive and a fundable Series-B profile. Accept a renegotiated deal (higher/cleaner price, founder secondary, reduced escrow, capped retention) if the motive is fair-value, the waterfall shows a life-changing largely-non-contingent founder outcome, and Series-B fundability is shaky. The one fact that most changes the answer is the acquirer's motive — get it before negotiating price. Residual uncertainty: acquirer motive, true deal structure, and Series-B fundability are all unknown today.
