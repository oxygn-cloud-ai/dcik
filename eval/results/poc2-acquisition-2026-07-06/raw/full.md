# FULL — Accept the $24M acquisition offer, or stay independent?

*Method: complete DCIK run (hardened v1.1.1). Inputs = LITE (Codex second-model pass + disconfirming research) PLUS the perspective library applied with decision-linkage, coverage ratchet, and quantitative-claim discipline: P0013 premise, P0018 incentive, P0008 counterparty, P0012 information-asymmetry, P0007 stakeholder, P0002 financial, P0011 second-order, P0010 temporal, P0015 bias.*

## Recommendation
**Do not accept as structured, and do not decline blindly. Convert the offer into a 3-to-4-week diligence-and-negotiation sprint whose output is a decision, not a deal. The single question that decides this — before any multiple analysis — is: *why does this acquirer want you now?* Confidence: HIGH that accepting the offer as-is would be a mistake; MEDIUM on the ultimate sell/stay call, because it hinges on three facts you don't yet have.**

## The three swing factors (everything else is secondary)
Decision-linkage — each is stated as *what would flip the recommendation*:

1. **Acquirer's real motive (P0008/P0018 — the load-bearing unknown).** If this is a defensive block, roadmap gap, acqui-hire, or customer/data grab, 6× is an *opening bid below strategic value* → **push hard or walk**. If they're pricing in something they see and you don't (churn, a coming competitor, category consolidation — P0012 information asymmetry: *why now?*), the offer may be *generous* → **lean accept**. You cannot tell which without diligencing their roadmap, account overlap, and build-vs-buy pressure. **This factor alone can invert the answer.**
2. **The real deal, not the headline (P0002).** "All-cash 6×" is not $24M certain. Middle-market deals routinely place 20–30% in escrow/earnout/retention, and earnouts realize only ~50–70% of maximum [sourced]. Demand a sources-and-uses schedule and a **cap-table waterfall at $24M** before trusting any per-founder figure — liquidation preferences, unvested equity, and option treatment can break the "55% → $13M" assumption. If ≥20–25% is contingent/retention, the effective price is well under 6×.
3. **Founder objective (P0013 — challenge the premise).** "Should we accept" presupposes exit is the goal. Whose goal — founders', company's, or investors'? Interests diverge (P0007): after Series-A preference, employees with options may net little; investors may want protection *or* a swing; founders get liquidity, possibly with handcuffs. Name the split; the "right" answer differs by seat.

## Coverage of the material considerations (ratchet — nothing dropped)
- **Valuation vs. alternatives (g1) / downside base rates (g2):** 6× looks light for 80% growth *only if that growth is efficient and defensible*. Against it: recent Series-A→B graduation is ~9–25% within two years [sourced] — the independent path's base rates are sobering, and the relevant comparison is the *probability-weighted, dilution- and time-adjusted* exit later, not "6× vs. a market multiple today."
- **Reversibility / optionality (g8):** selling is near-irreversible, but a bad Series B (down-round, heavy preferences, lost board control) is *also* a one-way door that can destroy founder optionality more severely than a clean exit. Optionality cuts both ways — don't over-weight it toward "stay."
- **Second-order (P0011):** an active sale process can chill the raise (investors read wavering commitment) and unsettle the team; a failed sale after shopping the company is a real cost. Sequence the market check quietly.
- **Timing (g10):** the Series-B bar has risen (capital efficiency, NRR, CAC payback, burn multiple); 80% growth alone won't clear it. Test fundability honestly *in parallel*, not after declining.
- **Founder psychology (P0015/g9):** the offer's salience is anchoring the whole framing. Separate company EV from founder personal utility — if fatigue or family liquidity is driving it, that's legitimate, but name it rather than dressing it as "the company should sell."
- **Tax:** QSBS eligibility, holding periods, and ordinary-income treatment of retention/earnout can swing founder *net* materially — model after-tax, not headline.

## Decision rule & what would change the answer
- **Push hard / walk** if diligence shows a strategic/defensive motive and a fundable Series-B profile (efficient growth, strong NRR).
- **Accept a renegotiated deal** (higher/cleaner price, founder secondary, reduced escrow, capped retention) if the motive is fair-value *and* the cap-table waterfall shows a genuinely life-changing, largely-non-contingent founder outcome *and* Series-B fundability is shaky.
- **The one fact that most changes the answer:** the acquirer's motive. Get it before negotiating price.
- **Residual uncertainty:** acquirer motive, true deal structure, and Series-B fundability are all unknown today; the recommendation is a process to resolve them in weeks, with HIGH confidence only on "don't sign this version now."
