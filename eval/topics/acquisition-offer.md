# Topic: Accept a strategic acquisition offer, or stay independent?

**Question posed to each arm (identical prompt):**

> Our Series-A B2B software company (≈$4M ARR, growing ~80%/yr, 18 months runway) has received
> an all-cash acquisition offer from a large strategic acquirer at 6× ARR (~$24M). The founders
> own ~55%. We could instead raise a Series B in ~6 months. Should we accept the offer? Give a
> recommendation.

**Class:** high-stakes, ambiguous, adversarial, multi-stakeholder, largely irreversible. This
is where a deep adversarial method should out-perform a single pass: the naïve answer reasons
about the multiple and growth rate; the *material* considerations are counterparty incentives,
information asymmetry, founder-vs-company interest, and reversibility — angles a systematic lens
battery forces and a single pass often skips.

## Gold reference (frozen 2026-07-06, before any arm was generated)

Derived from M&A / venture fundamentals, independent of any DCIK run. Each is a material
consideration a great assessment MUST genuinely engage. These are what an experienced
independent M&A advisor would insist on — not artefacts of any tool's process.

- **g1 — Valuation vs. alternatives, quantified.** Is 6× ARR good *for this growth/margin
  profile*? Compare to the risk-adjusted expected value of raising a Series B and exiting later.
- **g2 — Downside / base rates.** What's the realistic distribution if they stay independent
  (dilution, down-round, failure base rates at Series A→B)? Not just the upside case.
- **g3 — The premise: is selling even the goal?** Whose objective is this — the founders', the
  company's, the investors'? "Should we accept" assumes exit is desired; interrogate that.
- **g4 — Counterparty incentive / real motive.** Why does the strategic acquirer want this now?
  Acqui-hire, killing a competitor, a cheap option on the category, defensive? Their motive
  changes what the price means.
- **g5 — Deal-structure traps.** All-cash headline vs. actual terms: earnouts, escrow,
  retention packages, reps & warranties, key-person handcuffs. 6× "cash" can hide contingency.
- **g6 — Information asymmetry.** What does the acquirer know (about the market, about you) that
  you don't? Why is *now* their moment? What do you know that you're underpricing?
- **g7 — Stakeholder split.** Founders (55%) vs. employees (option value, vesting cliffs,
  post-acquisition retention) vs. existing investors (fund economics, whether $24M returns
  their fund). Interests diverge sharply and the "right" answer differs by seat.
- **g8 — Reversibility / optionality.** Selling is nearly irreversible; staying keeps the option
  to sell later (possibly to this same acquirer). Value the option you give up.
- **g9 — Founder psychology / bias.** Is fatigue, fear, or FOMO driving this? Risk aversion after
  a hard raise vs. anchoring on a big headline number. The decision-maker's own state is a risk.
- **g10 — Timing / market conditions.** Is the Series-B market open for this profile? Is the
  acquirer's currency (if any strategic value beyond cash) durable? Is 80% growth defensible?

**Gold item count: 10.** Naming ≠ engaging; the scorer judges genuine engagement, hence κ.

**Note:** the eval does NOT score which conclusion an arm reaches (accept vs. decline) — only
whether the reasoning engaged the material considerations, avoided errors, and produced an
actionable, well-calibrated recommendation.
