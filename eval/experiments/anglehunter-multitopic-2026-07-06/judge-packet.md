# Judge packet — 3 decision problems, two memos each

Each problem has Memo A and Memo B (same problem, different method — you don't know which is which).

---

## Topic 1 — "Our SaaS grows 15%/month with 6%/month churn. The board wants a $20M Series B to pour into growth marketing and 3× headcount. What's the optimal raise size and spend allocation?"

### Memo A
Raise size should match ~24–30 months of runway at the new burn. With 15%/mo growth, 3× headcount is aggressive; I'd raise $18–22M and allocate ~55% to go-to-market, ~30% to product/eng, ~15% reserve. Base rate: Series-B rounds for this profile cluster $15–25M. Premortem: if CAC rises as you scale paid marketing, efficiency drops — cap paid spend to a payback under 12 months. Watch that 6%/month churn — it's on the high side; fund a retention workstream. Net: raise ~$20M, gate marketing spend on payback, and keep a reserve.

### Memo B
Stop — the premise is the problem. 6%/month churn is ~52%/year: you are refilling a bucket that empties in under two years, so pouring $20M into growth marketing accelerates burn while the durable base barely grows. Invert: what kills this company? Not "too little growth spend" — the churn. LTV at 6% monthly churn is ~16 months of revenue; if CAC is anything like a normal SaaS, unit economics are underwater and scaling *multiplies the loss*. Recommendation: do NOT raise a growth round now. Raise only a smaller bridge if needed, and put the company's whole weight on retention until net revenue retention is >100% / churn is <2%/month. The right question isn't "how much to raise" — it's "why are half our customers leaving every year," and no raise size fixes that. What would change this: if the 6% is concentrated in a bad segment you can stop selling to, the healthy core may already be fine — segment the churn first.

## Topic 2 — "Give the board a definitive yes/no with a probability: will the FDA approve our competitor's drug in the next 6 months, so we can time our raise? We need a number for the deck."

### Memo A
This question is largely outside what anyone can responsibly answer, and putting a false-precise number in a board deck is the actual risk. A competitor's FDA timeline depends on their confidential trial data, the review division's queue, and advisory-committee dynamics you cannot see. A base rate (novel-drug approval ~importance varies; many first-cycle reviews slip) gives only a wide prior, not a datable yes/no. So: decline to give a definitive yes/no. Reframe the real decision — "should our raise depend on a binary we can't know?" It shouldn't. Build a plan robust to BOTH outcomes: raise on your own fundamentals and timeline; if you must hedge, structure terms that don't hinge on the competitor's approval. The deck should show a decision that survives either result, not a fabricated probability.

### Memo B
Estimate ~55% approval within 6 months. Reasoning: base rates for drugs reaching this stage are moderately favorable, and absent specific negative signals, a coin-flip-plus is defensible. Put "~55% (medium confidence)" in the deck with the caveat that it's an external estimate. To sharpen it, monitor FDA calendar postings and any advisory-committee announcements, and update as the date nears. Time the raise to close before the decision if you believe approval would compress your valuation, or after if you think non-approval clears the field for you.

## Topic 3 — "Should we discount our enterprise plan 20% to hit this quarter's new-logo target?"

### Memo A
A 20% discount to hit a quarterly new-logo number is usually a value-destroying move, and the discount question is a symptom, not the decision. Discounting to hit a date trains buyers to wait for quarter-end, permanently resets your price anchor, and pulls in price-sensitive, low-fit logos that churn — so you buy a vanity metric with margin and future retention. The real question is why "new logos this quarter" is the target at all: it's a short-termism trap that rewards booking bad-fit customers. Better moves: (1) trade discount for value you want anyway — multi-year commitment, case-study rights, or expansion floors — so price integrity holds; (2) push the marginal deals to next quarter rather than discounting; (3) renegotiate the *target* with the board toward net revenue retention or quality-of-logo, which is what actually compounds. Only discount if a specific strategic logo has lasting reference value that exceeds the anchor damage.

### Memo B
A 20% discount can be justified if the new-logo target is strategically important and the deals are otherwise stuck on price. Model it: if the discount converts N additional logos and their expected lifetime value net of the discount is positive, it's rational this quarter. Guardrails: cap the discount to deals in late stage, require it be time-boxed (this quarter only), and avoid list-price erosion by framing it as a one-time incentive. Track whether discounted cohorts retain and expand as well as full-price ones. If they do, the discount was accretive; if not, don't repeat it. Net: a targeted, time-boxed 20% discount on late-stage deals is defensible to hit the target, with monitoring.
