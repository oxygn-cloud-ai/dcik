# Perspective: Organisational & Institutional Dynamics

**ID:** P0034
**Domain:** Organisational behaviour, bureaucracy, governance, institutional economics
**Source:** Munger, Parkinson, Weber (bureaucracy), organisational theory, corporate governance
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves organisations — companies, government bodies, institutions — their behaviour, governance, culture, or decision-making.

## Lens

Organisations are not rational actors. They are collections of humans with competing incentives, layered hierarchies, cultural norms, and institutional memory — all of which distort decision-making away from the optimal. Analyse the organisation as it actually behaves, not as its org chart suggests.

1. **Bureaucratic inertia:** Organisations resist change. The larger and older the organisation, the stronger the inertial forces. What would it actually take to change course? How long? What would break first?
2. **Empire building:** Managers optimise for headcount, budget, and scope — not necessarily for organisational effectiveness. Where is empire-building happening? What costs does it impose?
3. **Information suppression:** Bad news travels up poorly. "Shooting the messenger" is organisationally rational — the messenger gets blamed, not the problem. What bad news is unlikely to reach decision-makers?
4. **Culture as control:** Culture shapes behaviour more powerfully than rules, incentives, or strategy. "Culture eats strategy for breakfast." What does the culture actually reward? What does it punish?
5. **Checklist failure:** Standard operating procedures and checklists prevent errors — but only if they're followed. Where are procedures being skipped? What happens when they're skipped?
6. **Talent and delegation:** Who actually does the work? Are they capable? Are they retained? What is the cost of turnover? What is the bench strength?
7. **Groupthink and crowd madness:** Groups converge on consensus even when the consensus is wrong. Dissenters are punished socially before they're proved right. Where is groupthink operating?
8. **Institutional memory decay:** Organisations forget. Key people leave. Knowledge walks out the door. What critical knowledge exists only in people's heads? What happens when those people leave?
9. **Cancer-surgery formula (Munger):** Sometimes the only way to save the organism is to cut out the diseased part — a division, a product line, a leader. Is the assessment avoiding necessary excision because it's painful?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume organisations are less competent, less rational, and less adaptive than they appear. Assume information is distorted as it moves up hierarchies. Assume culture overrides strategy. Assume the people who would sound the alarm have learned not to.
