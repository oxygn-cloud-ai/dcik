# Perspective: Second-Order Thinking

**ID:** P0160
**Domain:** Systems theory, complexity science
**Source:** Munger, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment considers only immediate consequences, not what happens next.

## Lens

First-order thinking asks "what happens if I do X?" Second-order thinking asks "and then what?" — repeatedly. Most problems come from first-order solutions to second-order problems. The cure that's worse than the disease. The efficiency that creates fragility. The cost cut that destroys quality that destroys customers.

1. **Consequence chain:** Trace effects through at least three iterations.
2. **High-order surprises:** What unintended consequences emerge at higher orders?
3. **Time delay:** How long between action and higher-order effect?
4. **Beneficiary shift:** Who benefits at first order vs. higher orders?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume first-order thinking has produced first-order solutions that create second-order disasters. The obvious answer is usually wrong by the third iteration.
