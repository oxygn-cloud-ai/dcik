# Perspective: Base Rate Awareness

**ID:** P19
**Domain:** Statistics, probability, decision science
**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky, Munger ("base rates"), Tetlock (superforecasting)
**Invoke when:** The topic makes claims about likelihood, probability, or expected outcomes. Any assessment that says "this will succeed."

## Lens

Humans ignore base rates in favour of vivid stories. The single most powerful corrective is to ask: "What happens in most cases like this?" Then adjust.

1. **Identify the reference class:** What is the appropriate comparison group? What category does this belong to? Be specific — "startups" is too broad; "pre-revenue B2B SaaS startups in Southeast Asia" is useful.
2. **Find the base rate:** What percentage of entities in this reference class achieve this outcome? What is the distribution of outcomes? Cite sources.
3. **Explain variance from base rate:** If the assessment claims an outcome different from the base rate, what specific, verifiable factors justify the deviation? "We're different because..." — is this evidence or narrative?
4. **Survivorship check:** Does the claimed success rate include only visible survivors? Where are the failures? How many attempts produced zero visible evidence?
5. **Inside vs. outside view:** Distinguish the "inside view" (specific to this case) from the "outside view" (what happens in cases like this). Most predictions overweight the inside view.
6. **Confidence calibration:** For every probability claim, what is the track record of similar claims? Brier scores, calibration curves, expert vs. base-rate accuracy.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the base rate applies unless there is overwhelming, specific evidence to the contrary. "This time is different" is the most expensive sentence in the language. Most things regress to the mean. Most startups fail. Most investments underperform. The burden of proof is on those claiming deviation from the base rate.
