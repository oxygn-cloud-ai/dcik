# Perspective: Cooperation & Interdependence

**ID:** P48
**Domain:** Game theory, cooperation, networks, ecosystems
**Source:** Axelrod (Evolution of Cooperation), Farnam Street, systems theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves partnerships, alliances, ecosystems, or any situation where outcomes depend on coordinated action by multiple parties.

## Lens

Competition gets the headlines. Cooperation builds the world. Most complex achievements — from supply chains to scientific discovery to infrastructure — require sustained cooperation among parties with partially aligned and partially conflicting interests. Analyse the cooperative dynamics.

1. **Cooperation inventory:** Who needs to cooperate for this to work? List every party whose coordinated action is required. What does each party need to do? What do they need from others?
2. **Incentive alignment for cooperation:** For each party: what do they gain from cooperating? What do they gain from defecting? Is the incentive structure stable or does it reward defection?
3. **Prisoner's dilemma detection:** Where are the situations where individual rationality leads to collectively worse outcomes? Price wars, arms races, tragedy of the commons, underinvestment in public goods. Are there mechanisms to escape the dilemma?
4. **Repeated game dynamics:** One-shot interactions reward defection. Repeated interactions reward cooperation — if the future matters enough. Do the parties expect to interact again? Is the shadow of the future long enough to sustain cooperation?
5. **Trust mechanisms:** What enables trust? Reputation systems, contracts, escrow, repeated interaction, transparency, third-party enforcement, social norms, aligned incentives. Which are present? Which are missing?
6. **Interdependence mapping:** Who depends on whom? Who is the critical node whose failure would cascade? Who has alternatives?
7. **Cooperation cost:** What does cooperation cost? Coordination overhead, information sharing, loss of autonomy, decision speed. Is the cost worth the benefit?
8. **Free-rider problem:** Who benefits from cooperation without contributing? How long can free-riding persist before cooperation collapses?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume cooperation is harder than it looks. Assume incentives favour defection in ways not yet visible. Assume the parties who need to cooperate have not fully internalised the costs of non-cooperation. Assume free-riders exist and are being ignored. Trust is earned in drops and lost in buckets.
