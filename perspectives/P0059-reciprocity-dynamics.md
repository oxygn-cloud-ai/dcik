# Perspective: Reciprocity Dynamics

**ID:** P0059
**Domain:** Social psychology, game theory, negotiation, relationship management
**Source:** Cialdini, Farnam Street, Munger (Reciprocation Tendency)
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves relationships, negotiations, partnerships, or any situation where past exchanges shape future behaviour.

## Lens

Reciprocity is one of the most powerful and universal human drives. We repay kindness with kindness, harm with harm, concession with concession. It operates below conscious awareness and across cultures. Every exchange creates a debt that the other party feels compelled to repay.

1. **Reciprocity inventory:** What debts exist between the parties? Favours given, concessions made, information shared, trust extended — each creates a reciprocity obligation. Who owes whom what?
2. **Reciprocity exploitation:** Is reciprocity being weaponised? The "free sample" technique (give something small, create obligation to buy), the "door-in-the-face" technique (make large request, get rejected, make smaller request — concession creates reciprocity), the "that's-not-all" technique. Is the assessment's subject using or being subjected to these?
3. **Negative reciprocity:** What harm has been done that demands repayment? Feuds, litigation, competitive retaliation, regulatory complaints — negative reciprocity cycles are self-reinforcing and hard to break. Where is a cycle operating?
4. **Reciprocity asymmetry:** Who is more sensitive to reciprocity? Some individuals and cultures are wired for strong reciprocity; others are transactional. Mismatched reciprocity expectations create conflict.
5. **Unasked-for favours:** What has been given without being requested? Unsolicited gifts create the strongest reciprocity obligations because they cannot be refused. Is the assessment's subject accepting unsolicited benefits without recognising the implied debt?
6. **Reciprocity decay:** How fast do reciprocity obligations fade? A favour done yesterday carries more weight than one done last year. What debts are expiring?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume reciprocity is operating even when parties deny it. Assume favours create obligations that will be collected. Assume negative reciprocity cycles are harder to break than they appear. The gift you didn't ask for is the most expensive one.
