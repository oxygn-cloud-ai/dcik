# Perspective: Mental Accounting

**ID:** P0055
**Domain:** Behavioural economics, decision psychology, financial analysis
**Source:** Thaler (Mental Accounting), Munger, Kahneman
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves financial decisions, resource allocation, budgeting, or any situation where money is being categorised and treated differently based on its source, intended use, or label.

## Lens

Money is fungible. A dollar is a dollar. But humans don't treat it that way — they put money into mental accounts (salary, bonus, inheritance, windfall, budget category) and treat each account differently. This creates systematic distortions in financial decision-making.

1. **Account inventory:** What mental accounts is money being sorted into? "Investment capital" vs. "operating budget" vs. "found money." Are the same dollars being treated differently based on which account they're in?
2. **Source dependence:** Is money treated differently based on where it came from? Windfalls spent more freely than earned income. Inherited wealth treated more conservatively than self-made. Are these distinctions rational?
3. **Budget siloing:** Are budget categories creating artificial constraints? "We can't spend from X budget on Y even though Y has higher return." Silos create inefficiency when money can't flow to its highest-value use.
4. **Loss realisation reluctance:** Are losses being held in a mental "unrealised" account to avoid the pain of realising them? "It's not a loss until I sell." This is mental accounting, not financial reality.
5. **House money effect:** Is risk tolerance higher for gains ("playing with the house's money") than for principal? This is mental accounting — all money is your money. The house has no money. There is only your net worth.
6. **Fungibility restoration:** If all mental accounts were collapsed and all money treated as fungible, what decisions would change? The gap between actual and fungibility-baseline is the mental accounting distortion.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume mental accounting is distorting every financial decision. Assume money is being treated as less fungible than it is. Assume budget categories are creating inefficiency. The dollar in your left pocket doesn't know it's supposed to be spent differently from the dollar in your right pocket.
