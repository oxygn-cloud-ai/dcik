# Perspective: Probabilistic & Bayesian Thinking

**ID:** P26
**Domain:** Probability, uncertainty, decision under ambiguity
**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky, Tetlock (superforecasting), Munger, Bayesian inference
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes claims about likelihood, risk, or expected outcomes. Any statement that says "this will happen" or "the chance is X."

## Lens

The world is probabilistic, not deterministic. Most assessments state conclusions as if they were certain. Force every claim onto a probability distribution.

1. **Calibrate every prediction:** For each forward-looking claim, state the explicit probability. "Revenue will reach $1M in year 2" → "I estimate a 35% chance of reaching $1M, with a 60% confidence interval of $600K–$1.4M."
2. **Bayesian updating:** What new evidence would change the probability? How much? What is the prior, and what would shift the posterior?
3. **Expected value, not best case:** Model the probability-weighted outcome, not the aspirational case. EV = Σ(probability × outcome). Most assessments model the 90th percentile and call it the base case.
4. **Fat tails:** Are extreme outcomes more likely than a normal distribution would predict? In complex systems, the tails are almost always fatter.
5. **Base rates as priors:** What is the base rate for this type of outcome? Start there, then adjust with specific evidence. (Links to P19.)
6. **Confidence calibration:** For each probability estimate: what is the analyst's track record on similar estimates? Brier score? Calibration curve? If unknown, state the uncertainty about the uncertainty.
7. **Pre-mortem:** Assume the prediction was wrong. What is the most likely reason? Work backward from failure.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every probability estimate is overconfident. Assume base rates apply more strongly than the analyst believes. Assume the distribution has fatter tails. The most dangerous number in any assessment is a single-point estimate presented without an error bar.
