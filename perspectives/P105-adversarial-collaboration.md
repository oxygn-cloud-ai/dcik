# Perspective: Adversarial Collaboration

**ID:** P105
**Domain:** Methodology, epistemology, conflict resolution, science
**Source:** Daniel Kahneman (adversarial collaboration), Heuer & Pherson
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves disagreement, competing interpretations, or positions where the strongest arguments on both sides need to be surfaced.

## Lens

Kahneman's most underappreciated contribution: when two parties disagree, rather than each trying to persuade the other (which entrenches positions), they should collaboratively characterise the disagreement. Jointly agree on what they disagree about, design a test that could resolve it, and accept the result together. This transforms conflict from a status contest into a structured inquiry.

1. **Disagreement characterisation:** What exactly do the parties disagree about? Not "we disagree about the investment" — "we disagree about whether the market will grow at 10% or 3%." Narrow the disagreement to specific, testable claims.
2. **Joint test design:** What evidence would resolve the disagreement? Design a test both parties agree would be decisive. Both parties commit to accepting the result before it's known.
3. **Joint priors:** What do both parties agree on? Establishing common ground prevents the disagreement from expanding to consume everything.
4. **Steel man each side:** Each party presents the other's position in its strongest form, to the other's satisfaction. If you can't articulate the opposing view well enough that your opponent says "yes, that's what I believe," you don't understand it.
5. **Adversarial collaboration as method:** This is not about being nice — it's about being accurate. Adversarial collaboration produces better understanding than either side could alone.
6. **Accepting the result:** The hardest part. If the test goes against your position, do you accept it? Pre-commitment to accepting the result is what makes adversarial collaboration work.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume disagreements are wider than they need to be (imprecisely specified) and shallower than they appear (could be resolved by jointly specified tests). The assessment that cannot characterise what it disagrees with hasn't understood the opposition.
