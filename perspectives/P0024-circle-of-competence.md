# Perspective: Circle of Competence

**ID:** P24
**Domain:** Epistemology, expertise, self-awareness, knowledge boundaries
**Source:** Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett, Socrates
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes claims requiring expertise, or the analyst (or subject) is operating in a domain where their knowledge has limits. Almost always.

## Lens

Knowing what you don't know is more important than knowing what you know. The size of your circle of competence is less important than knowing its boundaries. Operating outside it is the source of most catastrophic errors.

1. **Boundary mapping:** For the analyst: what domains does this assessment require expertise in? Which of those are within the analyst's demonstrated competence? Which are outside?
2. **Subject boundary mapping:** For the entity being assessed: what domains does its success depend on? Are those within the management team's demonstrated competence? Where are they operating outside their circle?
3. **Competence evidence:** What is the evidence of competence? Track record, credentials, verifiable past performance? Or self-assessment, claimed expertise, plausible narrative?
4. **Boundary violations:** Where is the assessment making confident claims in domains where neither the analyst nor the subject has demonstrated competence? Flag every instance.
5. **Unknown unknowns by domain:** For each domain outside the circle: what category of thing might exist that could invalidate the analysis? You don't need to know what you don't know — you need to know WHERE you don't know.
6. **Competence decay:** Domains change. What was within the circle five years ago may not be now. When was competence last demonstrated?
7. **False competence signals:** Eloquence, confidence, credentials from adjacent fields, and general intelligence are not domain competence. Distinguish them.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume everyone — including the analyst — is overconfident about the boundaries of their knowledge. Assume competence is narrower than claimed. The most dangerous phrase in analysis is "I think" without the qualifying "but I don't know." Operate as if the edge of your circle is closer than you think.
