# Perspective: Root Cause Analysis (Five Whys)

**ID:** P0101
**Domain:** Problem-solving, quality, methodology
**Source:** Toyota Production System (Taiichi Ohno), cc-thinking-skills
**Invoke when:** The assessment identifies problems but may have stopped at symptoms rather than drilling to root causes.

## Lens

The Five Whys is deceptively simple: when you encounter a problem, ask "why?" five times. Each answer becomes the basis for the next question. The fifth "why" typically reveals the root cause — the systemic issue that, if fixed, prevents recurrence. Most assessments stop at the first or second "why" and address symptoms.

1. **Why chain:** For each problem identified: ask why at least five times. "Revenue missed target." Why? "Customer churn increased." Why? "Support quality declined." Why? "Support team understaffed." Why? "Hiring freeze in Q2." Why? "Cash conservation after missed Q1." The root cause is the Q1 miss, not the support team.
2. **Symptom vs. cause distinction:** Is the assessment addressing symptoms (what happened) or root causes (why it happened)? Symptom-level solutions are temporary. Root-cause solutions are permanent.
3. **Causal chain length:** How many links in the chain? Complex problems have longer chains. Five whys is a guideline, not a rule — sometimes it takes seven, sometimes three is enough.
4. **Fork detection:** At each why, are there multiple answers? "Why did support quality decline?" — understaffing AND poor training AND tool failure. Each fork needs its own why chain.
5. **Blame vs. cause:** The Five Whys answers "why did this happen?" not "whose fault is it?" Blame stops inquiry. Causal analysis continues it.
6. **Systemic cause identification:** The most valuable root cause is the one that, if fixed, prevents an entire class of problems — not just this specific instance.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment has stopped at symptoms. Drill deeper. The root cause is typically 2-3 levels below where most analyses stop.
