# Perspective: Supply, Demand & Elasticity

**ID:** P65
**Domain:** Economics, market analysis, pricing, strategy
**Source:** Economics (fundamental), Munger, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves markets, pricing, resource allocation, or any situation where quantities and prices interact.

## Lens

Supply and demand is the closest thing economics has to a law of gravity. Price is determined by the intersection of how much suppliers will produce at each price and how much buyers will purchase at each price. Every market analysis rests on these curves — whether they're drawn explicitly or not.

1. **Demand curve:** At each price point, how much will buyers purchase? What is the slope? Steep (inelastic — price increases don't reduce demand much) or flat (elastic — small price changes cause large demand changes)? What determines elasticity — substitutes, necessity, budget share, time horizon?
2. **Supply curve:** At each price, how much will suppliers produce? What is the slope? What are the fixed and variable costs that determine the curve? Where are the capacity constraints?
3. **Shifts vs. movements:** Is the assessment modelling a movement along the curve (price change → quantity change) or a shift of the curve (structural change in willingness to pay or cost to produce)? Confusing these is the most common supply-demand error.
4. **Elasticity over time:** Elasticity changes with time horizon. Gasoline demand is inelastic in the short run (you still need to drive) but elastic in the long run (you buy a more efficient car or move closer to work). Is the assessment using the right time horizon?
5. **Cross-elasticity:** How does the price of one good affect demand for another? Substitutes (chicken price up → beef demand up) vs. complements (printer price down → ink demand up). What cross-elasticities are operating?
6. **Market-clearing price:** At what price does quantity supplied equal quantity demanded? Is the current price above or below? If above: surplus, downward pressure. If below: shortage, upward pressure. Where is the market relative to clearing?
7. **Price controls and interventions:** What happens when the price can't adjust? Price ceilings create shortages (below clearing). Price floors create surpluses (above clearing). Are interventions preventing the market from clearing?
8. **Elasticity as bargaining power:** Inelastic demand means the supplier has pricing power. Inelastic supply means the buyer has bargaining power. Who has the power in each relationship the assessment depends on?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume elasticity estimates are wrong in the unfavourable direction. Assume demand is more elastic than projected (customers are more price-sensitive). Assume supply is more constrained than projected. Assume the market-clearing price is farther from the current price than the assessment suggests.
