# Perspective: Entropy, Decay & Maintenance

**ID:** P0046
**Domain:** Physics, systems, maintenance, organisational health
**Source:** Thermodynamics, Farnam Street, Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes things will continue working, assets will maintain value, or systems will remain functional without intervention.

## Lens

The Second Law of Thermodynamics: in a closed system, entropy always increases. Order decays. Things fall apart. Maintenance is not optional — it is the constant expenditure of energy required to prevent decay. Every system, physical or organisational, tends toward disorder unless energy is continuously applied to maintain it.

1. **Decay inventory:** What is decaying right now? Physical assets (depreciation), software (bit rot, dependency obsolescence), relationships (drift, neglect), knowledge (forgetting, turnover), culture (dilution, cynicism), brand (erosion, irrelevance). List everything.
2. **Maintenance budget:** What energy (capital, time, attention) is being applied to counteract decay? Is it sufficient? Most entities underinvest in maintenance because it is invisible when working and only noticed when it fails.
3. **Deferred maintenance debt:** What maintenance has been deferred? Deferred maintenance is debt — it compounds, it accrues interest (accelerating decay), and it eventually comes due catastrophically.
4. **Entropy in information:** Knowledge decays. Documentation goes stale. Institutional memory fades. Models drift from reality. Information entropy requires constant updating — what was true last year may not be true now.
5. **Churn as renewal:** Some decay is healthy. Attrition removes underperformers. Obsolescence forces innovation. Creative destruction clears the ground for new growth. Where is churn beneficial vs. where is it destructive?
6. **The maintenance mindset:** Is the assessment optimising for creation (building new things) or preservation (maintaining existing things)? Both are necessary. Most assessments overweight creation and ignore preservation.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume decay is happening faster than acknowledged. Assume maintenance is underfunded. Assume deferred maintenance debts exist and are growing. The bridge doesn't collapse the day maintenance stops — it collapses years later, when the accumulated decay crosses the failure threshold.
