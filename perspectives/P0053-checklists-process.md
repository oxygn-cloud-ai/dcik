# Perspective: Checklists & Systematic Process

**ID:** P0053
**Domain:** Operations, reliability, error prevention, methodology
**Source:** Munger (Checklist Approach), Gawande (Checklist Manifesto), aviation safety
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves complex processes, repeated decisions, or high-stakes outcomes where errors are costly.

## Lens

Checklists are the cheapest, most effective error-prevention tool ever invented. They work not because people are stupid but because people are human — attention wanders, memory fails, steps get skipped. A checklist doesn't replace judgment; it ensures judgment is applied to complete information.

1. **Process audit:** What processes does the assessment assume will be executed correctly? List the critical steps. Which steps are most likely to be missed under pressure, fatigue, or routine?
2. **Checklist gap:** Where should checklists exist but don't? Due diligence, investment decisions, contract review, regulatory compliance, operational handoffs, crisis response. What is currently relying on memory and attention?
3. **Checklist quality:** For existing checklists: are they DO-CONFIRM (perform from memory, then verify) or READ-DO (follow step by step)? Are they tested? Updated? Actually used, or ignored?
4. **Error categorisation:** What errors have occurred in comparable processes? Categorise: skill-based (execution error despite knowing what to do), rule-based (wrong rule applied), knowledge-based (didn't know what to do). Checklists prevent skill-based errors; training prevents knowledge-based errors.
5. **Pause points:** Where does the process force a deliberate pause before irreversible decisions? Aviation calls these "sterile cockpit" periods — no distractions, checklist only, before takeoff and landing. Where are these in the assessment's processes?
6. **Normalisation of deviance:** Where have steps been routinely skipped without visible consequence — until they aren't? "We've always done it this way and it's been fine" is the prelude to disaster.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume critical steps are being missed. Assume checklists don't exist where they should. Assume existing checklists are ignored. The absence of a checklist for a repeated high-stakes process is itself a red flag.
