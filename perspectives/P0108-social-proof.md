# Perspective: Social-Proof Tendency

**ID:** P0108
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger (Social-Proof Tendency), Cialdini
**Invoke when:** The assessment references consensus, market trends, or what others are doing as evidence.

## Lens

1. **Consensus audit:** What does the assessment treat as true because others treat it as true? "Everyone agrees the market will grow at X%." "Industry consensus is Y." Consensus is not evidence.
2. **Information cascade:** Are people believing something because earlier people believed it, not because of independent analysis? Cascades form when each person assumes the crowd knows something they don't.
3. **Pluralistic ignorance:** Does the group publicly endorse a view that individuals privately doubt? "Everyone in the room thinks this is a bad idea but no one says so."
4. **Social proof in uncertainty:** Social proof is strongest when people are uncertain. In genuinely ambiguous situations, following others is rational. When is the uncertainty genuine and when is it manufactured by the desire for safety in numbers?
5. **Contrarian signal:** What would the analysis conclude if social proof were inverted — if the consensus view were treated as suspect rather than reliable?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume social proof is substituting for evidence wherever a consensus is cited. The fact that everyone believes something is evidence about the psychology of crowds, not about the truth of the belief.
