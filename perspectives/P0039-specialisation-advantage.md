# Perspective: Specialisation & Comparative Advantage

**ID:** P0039
**Domain:** Economics, strategy, organisational design, trade
**Source:** Ricardo (comparative advantage), Smith (division of labour), Munger, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves organisational design, strategic focus, make-vs-buy decisions, or competitive positioning.

## Lens

Specialisation creates wealth. The division of labour — each person, firm, or country doing what they do relatively best and trading for the rest — is the engine of economic progress. Most entities try to do too much themselves. This perspective asks: what should be specialised, and what is the cost of not specialising?

1. **Core competence audit:** What does the entity do that no one else can do as well? What is genuinely distinctive? What is merely "we've always done this"?
2. **Comparative advantage:** Even if the entity is the best at everything (impossible), it should still specialise in what it does *relatively* best and trade for the rest. Where is comparative advantage being ignored?
3. **Outsourcing candidates:** What activities could be done better, cheaper, or faster by specialists? What is the cost — in quality, time, and management attention — of doing them in-house?
4. **Focus cost:** What is the cost of the current scope? Every additional activity adds coordination overhead, dilutes management attention, and increases organisational complexity. What could be shed?
5. **Depth vs. breadth:** Going deep means dominating a narrow domain. Going broad means competing everywhere but winning nowhere. Which strategy is the entity pursuing? Is it working?
6. **Specialisation fragility:** Extreme specialisation creates extreme efficiency — and extreme fragility. If the specialist's niche disappears, the specialist disappears with it. What is the diversification hedge?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume most entities are over-diversified and under-specialised. Assume activities done in-house could be done better by specialists. Assume management attention is the scarcest resource and is being spread too thin. Focus creates value; breadth dilutes it.
