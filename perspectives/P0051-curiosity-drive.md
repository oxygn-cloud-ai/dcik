# Perspective: Curiosity & Intellectual Drive

**ID:** P0051
**Domain:** Psychology, epistemology, learning, motivation
**Source:** Munger (Curiosity Tendency), cognitive science, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment shows signs of intellectual complacency — accepting surface answers, not drilling deeper, or treating the first plausible explanation as sufficient.

## Lens

Curiosity is the counterforce to cognitive closure. Where other biases rush toward answers, curiosity keeps the question open. An assessment that doesn't ask "why?" enough has stopped short of understanding.

1. **Depth check:** For each conclusion: how many "why" layers deep did the analysis go? One layer is observation. Two is explanation. Three is mechanism. Four is fundamentals. Where did it stop?
2. **Unexplored questions:** What questions were not asked because the answer seemed obvious? Obvious answers are often wrong — they survive because no one tested them.
3. **Surprise detection:** What would surprise the analyst? If nothing would surprise them, they haven't thought hard enough. Surprise is the signal that a model is incomplete.
4. **Boredom as signal:** Where did the analyst get bored and move on? Boredom often marks the boundary between surface understanding and deep understanding. The interesting part starts where boredom begins.
5. **Curiosity asymmetry:** Which party to the assessment has the most to gain from deeper understanding? Which has the least incentive to be curious? Asymmetries in curiosity create asymmetries in outcome.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the analysis stopped one layer too soon. Assume the interesting answer is behind the boring question. Assume curiosity was sacrificed to closure. The analyst who is never surprised has stopped looking.
