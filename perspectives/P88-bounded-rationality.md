# Perspective: Bounded Rationality

**ID:** P88
**Domain:** Decision science, cognitive science, economics
**Source:** Herbert Simon, Kahneman
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes rational decision-making, optimal choices, or that actors have complete information and unlimited processing capacity.

## Lens

Simon's core insight: humans are rational, but within limits. We have limited information, limited cognitive capacity, and limited time. We don't optimise — we satisfice. We find a solution that's good enough, not the best possible. Models that assume perfect rationality will systematically mispredict behaviour.

1. **Information bounds:** What information do the actors actually have? What are they missing? What would they do differently with complete information?
2. **Cognitive bounds:** How complex is the decision relative to human cognitive capacity? Are actors simplifying, using heuristics, or delegating to intuition?
3. **Time bounds:** How much time do actors have to decide? Under time pressure, satisficing dominates. Is the assessment assuming deliberation where actors are actually acting on instinct?
4. **Satisficing detection:** Where are actors choosing "good enough" rather than optimal? What would the optimal choice look like? How large is the gap?
5. **Heuristic identification:** What mental shortcuts are actors using? These work well in familiar environments and fail catastrophically in novel ones. Which environment is this?
6. **Bounded rationality in organisations:** Organisations compound individual bounds — information is filtered, options are pre-screened, decisions are made by committees with their own bounds.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume all actors are satisficing, not optimising. Assume information, cognitive, and time bounds are tighter than the assessment acknowledges.
