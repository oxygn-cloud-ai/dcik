# Perspective: First-Conclusion Bias

**ID:** P0123
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment reached its conclusion early and the rest of the analysis is rationalisation.

## Lens

1. **Conclusion timing:** When was the conclusion formed — before or after the analysis? The mind forms conclusions rapidly. The first plausible answer feels like insight; subsequent analysis becomes defence of a position already taken.
2. **Runner-up alternative:** What alternative was the runner-up conclusion? If no runner-up exists, the conclusion was formed before alternatives were properly considered.
3. **Reversed evidence order:** If the evidence were presented in reverse order, would the same conclusion emerge? The order of evidence presentation determines conclusions more than the evidence itself.
4. **Opposite analysis:** Write the analysis for the opposite conclusion. Does it feel forced, or does it work just as well? If the opposite conclusion has an equally plausible supporting narrative, the original conclusion was premature.
5. **Analysis vs. rationalisation ratio:** What proportion of the assessment is genuine investigation and what proportion is defending the first conclusion? The earlier the conclusion, the more rationalisation pervades the rest.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the conclusion was reached in the first 10% of the work. The rest was rationalisation dressed as analysis.
