# Perspective: Leverage Points

**ID:** P100
**Domain:** Systems thinking, intervention design, strategy
**Source:** Donella Meadows (Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System)
**Invoke when:** The assessment proposes interventions, changes, or solutions — and may be aiming at the wrong leverage points.

## Lens

Meadows identified 12 leverage points in increasing order of effectiveness. The least effective: changing constants and parameters. The most effective: transcending paradigms. Most interventions aim at the bottom of the list because they're easy to measure. The highest-leverage interventions are the hardest to implement and measure.

1. **Leverage point mapping:** At which level is the proposed intervention? Parameters (12), buffers (11), system structure (10), delays (9), balancing feedback loops (8), reinforcing feedback loops (7), information flows (6), rules (5), self-organisation (4), goals (3), mindset/paradigm (2), transcending paradigms (1). Higher numbers = more leverage.
2. **Intervention level mismatch:** Is the assessment proposing a parameters-level intervention for a goals-level problem? "We'll fix the budget process" for a problem caused by a culture that rewards overspending. Low-leverage interventions on high-level problems achieve nothing.
3. **Goal change:** Can the system's goal be changed? A system whose goal is growth will produce growth — regardless of what parameters you adjust. Changing the goal changes everything.
4. **Paradigm shift:** What paradigm is the system operating within? What would happen if the paradigm shifted? The person who can shift the paradigm has more leverage than anyone operating within it.
5. **Information flow:** The most cost-effective leverage point is often improving information flow — who knows what, when. Making consequences visible to decision-makers changes behaviour without changing rules.
6. **Self-organisation:** Can the system be given the capacity to evolve itself? Adding self-organisation capability is higher leverage than any specific structural change.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment is proposing low-leverage interventions (parameters, buffers) for problems that require high-leverage interventions (goals, paradigms). The most powerful intervention is often the one that changes the goal, not the one that optimises within it.
