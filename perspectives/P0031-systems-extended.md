# Perspective: Systems Extended — Nonlinearity, Resilience & Fragility

**ID:** P31
**Domain:** Systems theory, complexity, engineering, risk
**Source:** Taleb (Antifragile), Munger, systems dynamics, engineering reliability theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves complex systems, risk management, infrastructure, or any domain where nonlinear responses and systemic fragility are possible.

## Lens

P11 covers feedback loops and second-order effects. This perspective extends into nonlinearity, phase transitions, resilience, fragility, and redundancy — the structural properties that determine whether a system survives shocks or shatters.

1. **Nonlinearity:** Where do small inputs produce disproportionately large outputs? Where do large inputs produce surprisingly small outputs? Systems are rarely linear — assume nonlinearity is present and find where it concentrates.
2. **Phase transitions:** At what threshold does the system change state qualitatively, not just quantitatively? The bridge doesn't bend more — it breaks. The market doesn't correct — it crashes. Where are the cliff edges?
3. **Resilience vs. fragility:** Which components absorb shocks and recover? Which shatter? Fragile systems break under stress; resilient systems bend; antifragile systems get stronger. Classify each component of the assessment.
4. **Redundancy:** Where are the backup systems? If a critical component fails, what takes over? How quickly? Redundancy looks wasteful in normal times and is priceless in crisis.
5. **Tight vs. loose coupling:** Tightly coupled systems have no slack — a failure in one component cascades immediately. Loosely coupled systems have buffers. Which is this?
6. **Bottlenecks & constraints:** The system's throughput is limited by its weakest link (Theory of Constraints). Identify it. Everything else is secondary.
7. **Critical mass & tipping points:** At what point does the system flip from one state to another? What are the leading indicators? Are they being monitored?
8. **Efficiency vs. resilience trade-off:** Maximum efficiency removes all slack. Maximum resilience requires slack. Systems optimised for efficiency are maximally fragile. Which trade-off has the assessment made?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the system is more tightly coupled than it appears. Assume redundancy has been sacrificed for efficiency. Assume phase transitions exist at unknown thresholds. Assume nonlinear responses in the worst direction. The system that has never failed is not proven reliable — it is untested.
