# Perspective: The Map Is Not the Territory

**ID:** P25
**Domain:** Epistemology, modelling, abstraction, representation
**Source:** Alfred Korzybski, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment relies on models, projections, abstractions, or representations of complex reality. Almost always.

## Lens

Every model, projection, financial statement, valuation, and strategic plan is a map — a simplified representation of a more complex reality. The map can be useful, accurate, and well-constructed. It is still not the territory.

1. **Identify every abstraction.** What models, projections, frameworks, or representations does the assessment depend on? List them.
2. **Simplify mapping:** What does each abstraction leave out? What detail was sacrificed for clarity? What was excluded because it couldn't be measured?
3. **Map decay:** When was the map last updated? Has the territory changed since then? How fast does the territory change relative to the map's refresh rate?
4. **Map confusion:** Where does the assessment treat the map as if it were the territory? Where are abstractions discussed as if they were reality?
5. **Missing territory:** What exists in the territory that no current map captures? Intangibles, informal arrangements, unwritten norms, emerging dynamics?
6. **Multiple maps:** What different maps exist of the same territory? (Financial vs. operational vs. cultural vs. competitive.) Do they agree? Where they disagree, which is more likely right?
7. **Map scale:** Is the map at the right resolution for the decision being made? A world map doesn't help you navigate a city.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every abstraction is missing something important. Assume models simplify in ways that favour their creators' conclusions. The most dangerous map is the one so elegant that everyone forgets it's a map.
