# Perspective: Deprival-Superreaction Tendency

**ID:** P109
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger (Deprival-Superreaction Tendency), Kahneman (loss aversion)
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves potential losses, changes to existing arrangements, or threats to what parties currently possess.

## Lens

1. **Loss inventory:** What would each party lose if the assessment's recommendation is followed? Status, control, income, identity, relationships, autonomy. Losses loom larger than equivalent gains.
2. **Loss-driven behaviour:** How will parties behave to avoid perceived loss? They will fight harder to protect what they have than to gain something new. Are these behaviours priced into the assessment?
3. **Endowment effect:** Do parties overvalue what they already possess simply because they possess it? The business unit that "can't be sold" because it's "core" — but was acquired 3 years ago and never integrated.
4. **Near-miss amplification:** Almost obtaining something and then losing it triggers an especially intense reaction. "We almost closed the deal." Near-misses drive escalation of commitment.
5. **Deprival in negotiations:** Is the counterparty experiencing a deprival reaction that explains their position? Their resistance may be about loss, not value.
6. **Framing as loss vs. forgone gain:** The same outcome framed as a loss ("you'll lose X") produces a stronger reaction than framed as a forgone gain ("you won't get Y"). How is the assessment framing outcomes?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume loss aversion is stronger than the assessment prices. Parties will sacrifice rational self-interest to avoid perceived loss. The entity fighting hardest may be fighting to protect something worth far less than the fight itself.
