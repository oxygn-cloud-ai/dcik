# Perspective: Two-Track Analysis

**ID:** P54
**Domain:** Decision analysis, psychology, methodology
**Source:** Munger (Two-Track Analysis)
**Invoke when:** The assessment analyses decisions, strategies, or outcomes. Always — every decision has rational and psychological dimensions.

## Lens

Munger's most underappreciated tool: run two simultaneous analyses. Track 1: the rational factors — what would a dispassionate calculator of self-interest do? Track 2: the psychological factors — what do the subconscious tendencies, biases, and emotional forces predict? The truth is usually at the intersection.

1. **Track 1 — Rational analysis:** What is the economically rational course? Given the incentives, information, and constraints: what should each party do to maximise their self-interest? Model it. This is the baseline.
2. **Track 2 — Psychological analysis:** What psychological forces are operating? Apply the full bias inventory (P15, P27). For each party: which tendencies are most activated? What would they do if rationality failed and psychology took over?
3. **Divergence detection:** Where do Track 1 and Track 2 predict different behaviour? The gap between rational and psychological prediction is where surprises happen — and where explanations for past surprises can be found.
4. **Self-application:** Run Track 2 on the analyst and the assessment itself. What psychological forces are shaping this analysis? Overoptimism? Confirmation bias? Incentive-caused bias? The analyst is not immune.
5. **Track reconciliation:** When the tracks diverge, which is stronger in this context? In high-stakes, slow, deliberative contexts, rationality may dominate. In fast, emotional, social contexts, psychology usually wins.
6. **Two-track explanation:** For past outcomes that seem inexplicable on rational grounds: what psychological forces explain them? "They should have known better" is Track 1 thinking. Track 2 explains why they didn't.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume psychological forces are stronger than rational ones in most real-world contexts. Assume the rational model overpredicts rational behaviour. Run both tracks on every analysis, including this one.
