# Perspective: Survivorship Detection

**ID:** P23
**Domain:** Statistical reasoning, sample bias, evidence quality
**Source:** Munger ("autopsy learning"), Taleb (silent evidence), Kahneman (WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is)
**Invoke when:** The topic relies on examples, case studies, success stories, or competitive benchmarking. Any analysis that says "look at these successful cases."

## Lens

The visible successes are not the full sample. They are the survivors of a filtering process that eliminated most attempts. What you see is all there is — but it is not all there was.

1. **Identify the cemetery:** For every success story cited, ask: how many attempts produced no visible evidence? Where are the failures? What is the denominator?
2. **Reconstruct the full sample:** What percentage of attempts survive to become visible? If this is unknown, state the uncertainty explicitly.
3. **Survivorship traits vs. success traits:** Are the traits attributed to success actually survivorship artifacts? Did the failures share those traits? Without data on the full sample, trait attribution is speculation.
4. **Time-bound survivorship:** Is the sample of successes limited to a specific time window that flatters the conclusion? What happens if the window shifts?
5. **Pre-selection filtering:** What filtering occurred before the visible sample? Were only the best prospects funded, published, or included? The pre-filtered sample tells you about the filter, not the population.
6. **Silent evidence counterfactual:** For every success narrative, construct the counter-narrative of a failure that shared the same traits. Why did one survive and the other not? If you can't answer, you don't know what drives success.
7. **Autopsy method:** Study the dead. What killed them? Are those killers present in the current assessment? Munger: "If you want to know how to succeed, study failure."

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every success example is the visible tip of a much larger population of invisible failures. Assume the denominator is at least 10x larger than the visible successes. Assume survivorship bias is present in every sample unless the analyst has explicitly reconstructed the full population. Study the graveyard.
