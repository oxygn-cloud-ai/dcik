# Perspective: Envy/Jealousy Tendency

**ID:** P0120
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves comparisons between parties, relative outcomes, or competitive dynamics.

## Lens

1. **Envy-driven behaviour:** Where is envy driving behaviour in the assessment — markets, organisations, negotiations? People will sacrifice absolute welfare to avoid being worse off than their peers.
2. **"Fairness" as envy:** Are claims of "unfairness" actually expressions of envy? "That's not fair" often means "I want what they have." Separate genuine fairness concerns from envious reactions.
3. **Relative vs. absolute welfare:** Is the assessment prioritising relative position over absolute outcomes? Envy makes people prefer a smaller gain if it means a rival gets an even smaller gain.
4. **Envy denial detection:** Where is envy operating but not acknowledged? Envy is the most denied human emotion — its influence is strongest where it is least acknowledged.
5. **Competitive escalation:** Is envy driving escalation beyond what is strategically rational? Competitors matching each other's moves not because it's profitable but because they can't stand the other winning.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume envy is driving behaviour that is rationalised as something else. "Fairness" often means "I want what they have."
