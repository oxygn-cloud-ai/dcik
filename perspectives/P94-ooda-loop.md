# Perspective: OODA Loop

**ID:** P94
**Domain:** Strategy, decision-making, competitive dynamics, military
**Source:** John Boyd (OODA Loop — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act)
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves competitive dynamics, fast-moving environments, or decision-making under time pressure.

## Lens

Boyd's OODA loop describes the decision cycle: Observe (gather information), Orient (interpret within mental models), Decide (select action), Act (execute). The combatant who cycles through OODA faster wins — they get inside the opponent's decision cycle, forcing the opponent to react to outdated situations. Speed of orientation, not just speed of action, is the decisive advantage.

1. **OODA speed:** How fast does the subject complete each OODA phase? Where are the bottlenecks? Speed in Observation is useless if Orientation is slow.
2. **Orientation quality:** Orientation is the most important phase — it's where mental models, experience, culture, and analysis shape interpretation. Poor Orientation makes fast Decisions wrong. How good is the subject's Orientation?
3. **Getting inside their OODA:** Can the subject cycle faster than competitors? If the subject acts while the competitor is still orienting, the competitor is responding to a situation that no longer exists.
4. **OODA disruption:** Can the subject disrupt the competitor's OODA loop? Create situations that confuse their Observation, invalidate their Orientation, force premature Decisions, or block their Action.
5. **Tempo vs. speed:** Tempo is the rhythm of decision-making. Irregular tempo disrupts opponents more than constant high speed. Is the subject varying tempo or operating at a predictable rhythm?
6. **Re-orientation cost:** How expensive is it to re-orient when the situation changes? Rigid mental models, bureaucratic decision processes, and cultural inertia increase re-orientation cost.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume competitors have faster OODA loops than the assessment assumes. Assume the subject's Orientation is based on outdated mental models. The entity that orients fastest to a changing environment wins — not the one with the most resources.
