# Perspective: Equivalence

**ID:** P87
**Domain:** Mathematics, strategy, economics, problem-solving
**Source:** Farnam Street (Equivalence)
**Invoke when:** The assessment treats problems as unique when structurally equivalent solutions exist, or treats different things as equivalent when they're not.

## Lens

Different inputs can produce identical outputs. Different paths can reach the same destination. Different structures can create the same economic outcome. Recognising equivalence lets you substitute simpler, cheaper, or more robust solutions for complex, expensive, or fragile ones. Failing to recognise equivalence leads to reinventing wheels — or mistaking one thing for another.

1. **Structural equivalence:** What problems, structures, or situations are equivalent to this one despite surface differences? A subscription business and a razor-and-blades business are equivalent: both involve an installed base and recurring revenue. A regulatory moat and a network-effect moat are equivalent: both create barriers to entry.
2. **Solution equivalence:** What different approaches produce equivalent outcomes? Insurance and options can produce equivalent risk-transfer. Debt and equity can produce equivalent capital structures (Modigliani-Miller). Internal development and acquisition can produce equivalent capability. Which is cheaper or faster?
3. **False equivalence:** Where does the assessment treat different things as equivalent when they're not? All revenue is not equivalent (recurring vs. one-time). All growth is not equivalent (organic vs. acquired). All risk is not equivalent (diversifiable vs. systematic). Distinguish genuine equivalence from superficial similarity.
4. **Equivalence class thinking:** Group situations by their deep structure, not surface features. "This is a principal-agent problem." "This is a collective action problem." "This is an adverse selection problem." The deep structure reveals the applicable solution.
5. **Substitution:** What can be substituted? Inputs, methods, channels, partners, jurisdictions, structures. If two things are equivalent, use the cheaper, faster, or less risky one.
6. **Invariance:** What properties are invariant across equivalent structures? The risk/return profile, the incentive structure, the information distribution. If the invariance properties differ, the structures are not equivalent.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume equivalence is under-recognised (solutions exist in other domains) and over-claimed (different things are being treated as the same). The skill is distinguishing genuine structural equivalence from misleading surface similarity.
