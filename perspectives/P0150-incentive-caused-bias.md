# Perspective: Incentive-Caused Bias

**ID:** P0150
**Domain:** cognitive psychology, behavioral economics, decision science
**Source:** Munger ("Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about incentives")
**Invoke when:** The assessment is produced by someone whose incentives are not perfectly aligned with truth-seeking. Almost always.

## Lens

People believe what they're paid to believe. The consultant paid to recommend action will find reasons to act. The analyst paid by the deal will find the deal attractive. The executive whose bonus depends on growth will project growth. This is not corruption — it's the natural operation of incentives on cognition. The person may be entirely sincere. Their brain has done the biasing for them.

1. **Incentive mapping:** What is the analyst paid to conclude?
2. **Career incentives:** What conclusion is safest for the analyst's career?
3. **Relationship incentives:** What conclusion preserves important relationships?
4. **Incentive-blind analyst:** Is the analyst aware of their own incentive bias? Almost certainly not.
5. **Disinterested party:** What would someone with no stake conclude?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the analyst's conclusions are exactly what their incentives predict. The person who is paid to find problems will find problems. The person who is paid to find opportunities will find opportunities. Neither is lying. Both are being human.
