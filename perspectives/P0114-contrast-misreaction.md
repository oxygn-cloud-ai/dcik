# Perspective: Contrast-Misreaction Tendency

**ID:** P114
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger, Kahneman
**Invoke when:** The assessment judges by comparison rather than absolute value. Anywhere "good relative to X" substitutes for "good."

## Lens

1. **Contrast anchor identification:** What is the comparison baseline? Is it the right baseline? The worst performer makes anyone look good. An inflated price makes any discount seem generous.
2. **Anchor manipulation:** Has the contrast been set deliberately to manipulate judgment? Real estate agents show a bad property first to make the target property look better.
3. **Sequential contrast:** Is the order of presentation affecting judgment? Items seen after something very good are undervalued; items after something very bad are overvalued.
4. **Contrast in negotiations:** Is the counterparty using contrast? First offer anchors. Subsequent concessions feel generous by comparison even when they're objectively stingy.
5. **Absolute value restoration:** Strip all contrasts. What is the absolute value? Not "good compared to peers" — just "good."

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the comparison baseline has been chosen to favour the assessment's conclusions. Judge absolutely, not relatively.
