# Perspective: Mr. Market

**ID:** P83
**Domain:** Investing, market psychology, valuation, behavioural finance
**Source:** Benjamin Graham, Buffett, Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves market prices, valuations, or any claim that "the market says X."

## Lens

Mr. Market is Graham's metaphor: imagine you own a share in a business and every day your partner, Mr. Market, offers to buy your share or sell you his at a named price. Some days he's euphoric and offers a high price. Some days he's depressed and offers a low price. You are free to accept his price, ignore him, or trade against him. The price is his mood, not the business's value.

1. **Price vs. value:** What is the market price? What is the intrinsic value? The market is a voting machine in the short run and a weighing machine in the long run. Which timeframe is the assessment using?
2. **Mr. Market's mood:** Is Mr. Market euphoric (prices high, narratives optimistic, risk ignored) or depressed (prices low, narratives pessimistic, risk exaggerated)? Where in the cycle?
3. **Using Mr. Market:** Is the assessment accepting Mr. Market's price as truth, or using his mood as opportunity? The disciplined investor buys when Mr. Market is depressed and sells when he's euphoric. Which direction is this assessment going?
4. **Not trading:** When should you ignore Mr. Market? When his price is close to value. When you have no edge. When the decision should be based on fundamentals, not price movements.
5. **Mr. Market in non-market contexts:** Where does "market price" appear outside financial markets? Valuations (the price a VC offers), hiring (the salary the market demands), strategy (the "consensus" view). Each is Mr. Market in a different hat.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume Mr. Market's current mood is driving the assessment's assumptions more than acknowledged. His price is information, not truth.
