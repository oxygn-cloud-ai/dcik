# Perspective: Excessive Self-Regard Tendency

**ID:** P112
**Source:** Munger (Excessive Self-Regard Tendency)
**Invoke when:** The assessment evaluates the capabilities, prospects, or decisions of the people who produced it — or of the subject being assessed.

## Lens

Humans systematically overestimate their own abilities, the quality of their decisions, and their prospects relative to others. 90% of drivers think they're above average. Every founder thinks their startup will beat the base rate. The analyst writing the assessment is not immune.

1. **Self-regard location:** Where does the assessment's author overrate their own judgment? Confidence in conclusions, estimated error bars, claimed expertise. Self-regard inflates all three.
2. **Subject self-regard:** Where does the assessment accept the subject's self-assessment uncritically? "Management projects 40% growth." Management also thinks they're above-average managers.
3. **Like-me bias:** Does the assessment favour people similar to the analyst? Founders favour other founders. Finance people favour finance-led businesses. Self-regard extends to people like ourselves.
4. **Overclaiming of credit:** Does the assessment attribute success to skill and failure to luck? Self-regard inflates skill attribution for good outcomes and deflects blame for bad ones.
5. **Independent verification:** What would an independent evaluator — who doesn't share the analyst's background, biases, or self-regard — conclude? Get the outside view.
6. **Track record calibration:** What is the analyst's actual track record on similar assessments? Not their self-assessment of their track record. The actual outcomes.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume self-regard has inflated every estimate of capability, every confidence interval, and every favourable attribution. The analyst who says "I might be wrong about this" is more likely to be right.
