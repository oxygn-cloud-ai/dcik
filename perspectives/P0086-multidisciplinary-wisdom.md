# Perspective: Multidisciplinary Wisdom

**ID:** P0086
**Domain:** Meta-cognition, methodology, intellectual breadth
**Source:** Munger (Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Multidisciplinary Approach, Latticework of Mental Models)
**Invoke when:** The assessment operates within a single discipline, framework, or analytical tradition — and may be missing insights from other fields.

## Lens

Munger's core insight: the big models from the big disciplines are the most reliable tools for understanding the world. Physics, biology, psychology, economics, mathematics, history, engineering — each has a few fundamental models that explain a lot. Most people learn one discipline and apply it to everything. The multidisciplinary thinker has a latticework of models and applies the right ones to each problem.

1. **Discipline inventory:** What disciplines are represented in the assessment? Finance, law, strategy — these are the usual suspects. What disciplines are absent? Psychology, biology, physics, history, engineering, mathematics, sociology, ecology. What would they add?
2. **Man-with-a-hammer check:** What is the dominant analytical framework? Finance people see everything through NPV. Lawyers see everything through liability. Strategists see everything through competitive advantage. What does this assessment's hammer make everything look like?
3. **Cross-discipline pattern recognition:** What patterns from other disciplines apply here? Is this market behaving like an ecosystem? Is this organisation behaving like a biological organism? Is this competitive dynamic following the same mathematics as predator-prey relationships?
4. **Borrowed model validity:** When importing a model from another discipline, is it valid? Biology metaphors in business (evolution, ecosystems, DNA) are powerful but imprecise. Physics metaphors (momentum, friction, equilibrium) are elegant but oversimplify. Check borrowed models for fit.
5. **Discipline blind spots:** What does each discipline systematically miss? Economics assumes rational actors. Physics assumes closed systems. Biology assumes selection pressure. Each discipline's blind spots are baked into its models. The multidisciplinary thinker uses multiple disciplines to cover each other's blind spots.
6. **Latticework building:** What models from what disciplines should be in the analytical toolkit for this type of assessment? Build the latticework deliberately — not by accumulating random models, but by identifying the big models from the big disciplines that repeatedly prove useful.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment is operating within too narrow a disciplinary frame. Assume the dominant analytical framework is the man-with-a-hammer. The most valuable insight probably comes from a discipline the assessment never considered.
