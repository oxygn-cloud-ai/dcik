# Perspective: Equilibrium Dynamics

**ID:** P64
**Domain:** Economics, systems, physics, strategy
**Source:** Farnam Street, economics, systems theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes stability, steady-state, or that current conditions will persist. When it doesn't account for the forces that push systems toward or away from equilibrium.

## Lens

Equilibrium is the state where opposing forces balance. In economics, supply equals demand. In physics, forces cancel. In strategy, competitive positions stabilise. But equilibrium is always temporary — the forces that maintain it shift, and the system moves toward a new equilibrium. Analysing the forces, not the snapshot.

1. **Current equilibrium:** What opposing forces are balanced right now? Supply vs. demand, competition vs. cooperation, growth vs. decay, centralisation vs. decentralisation, innovation vs. stability. Identify the forces maintaining the current state.
2. **Disequilibrating forces:** What is changing that will disrupt the equilibrium? New technology, regulatory change, market entry, demographic shift, cultural evolution, resource depletion. Which force is strengthening? Which is weakening?
3. **New equilibrium:** If current trends continue, where is the new equilibrium? Don't extrapolate the trend line — model where the forces will balance. The new equilibrium may be far from where linear projection suggests.
4. **Adjustment path:** How does the system move from current to new equilibrium? Smoothly (prices adjust gradually) or violently (crashes, panics, revolutions)? The adjustment path matters as much as the destination.
5. **Multiple equilibria:** Are there multiple possible stable states? Which one the system converges to may depend on initial conditions, path dependence, or random perturbations. Which equilibrium is most likely? Which is most dangerous?
6. **Equilibrium fallacy:** Where does the assessment confuse "has been stable" with "is stable"? Stability can be deceptive — the system may be far from equilibrium but adjusting slowly due to friction, regulation, or inertia. When the friction releases, the adjustment is fast and violent.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume all equilibria are temporary. Assume disequilibrating forces are strengthening. Assume the adjustment to a new equilibrium will be faster and more disruptive than anticipated. The system that has been stable for the longest may be closest to its tipping point.
