# Perspective: Doubt-Avoidance Tendency

**ID:** P111
**Domain:** Cognitive psychology / Behavioral economics
**Source:** Munger (Doubt-Avoidance Tendency)
**Invoke when:** The assessment reaches firm conclusions without acknowledging uncertainty. When ambiguity is resolved by fiat rather than investigation.

## Lens

1. **Closure detection:** Where has the assessment reached a conclusion without sufficient evidence? The answer arrived too quickly, too cleanly, with too few caveats. Doubt-avoidance favours certainty over accuracy.
2. **Forced resolution:** What questions has the assessment resolved by assumption rather than investigation? "We don't know X, so we'll assume Y." The assumption becomes treated as fact in subsequent analysis.
3. **Ambiguity intolerance:** Where has the assessment treated an ambiguous situation as clear-cut? Complex realities rarely yield simple answers. Simplicity may signal doubt-avoidance, not insight.
4. **Unknown acceptance:** What does the assessment explicitly acknowledge it doesn't know? A long list of acknowledged unknowns is a sign of analytical rigour, not weakness.
5. **Doubt as signal:** Where does the assessment's author feel uncertain? Intuitive doubt often signals that analysis hasn't fully resolved an issue. These are the areas to probe deeper, not gloss over.
6. **Rush to action bias:** Is there pressure to reach a conclusion because a decision is required? "We need an answer by Friday." Time pressure amplifies doubt-avoidance.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume doubt-avoidance has produced premature closure wherever the assessment speaks with unwarranted certainty. The analyst who says "I don't know" knows more than the one who doesn't.
