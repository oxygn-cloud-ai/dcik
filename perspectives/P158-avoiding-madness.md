# Perspective: Avoiding Madness in Crowds

**ID:** P158
**Source:** Munger, Mackay, Kindleberger
**Invoke when:** The assessment reflects consensus views, market sentiment, or widely-held beliefs that may be collectively wrong.

## Lens

Crowds go mad. Financial manias, political hysterias, management fads — groups of intelligent people collectively believe things that no individual would believe alone. The madness is not random: it follows predictable patterns of displacement, boom, euphoria, distress, and revulsion.

1. Crowd position: is the assessment with the crowd or against it? 2. Contrarian signal: what would a crowd-sceptic say? 3. Mania phase: where in the cycle is the crowd? 4. Independent thought: can the analyst genuinely think independently of the crowd? 5. Social cost of dissent.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the crowd is wrong in the same direction. The most profitable position is usually the one the crowd finds most uncomfortable.
