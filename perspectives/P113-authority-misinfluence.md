# Perspective: Authority-Misinfluence Tendency

**ID:** P113
**Source:** Munger, Milgram
**Invoke when:** The assessment cites experts, authorities, or credentials as evidence — rather than evaluating the underlying arguments.

## Lens

Humans defer to authority — sometimes appropriately (surgeons know more about surgery), sometimes disastrously (following orders to harm). Authority substitutes for independent judgment: "Expert X says Y" becomes "Y is true." The more prestigious the authority, the less scrutiny their claims receive.

1. **Authority citation audit:** Where does the assessment cite authority instead of evidence? "According to McKinsey..." "As Professor X argues..." Authority adds weight but not truth.
2. **Authority domain check:** Is the cited authority actually an authority in the relevant domain? A Nobel economist may be useless on organisational psychology. Authority is domain-specific.
3. **Authority incentives:** What are the cited authority's incentives? Consultants paid to produce certain findings. Academics incentivised to publish novel results. Regulators incentivised to appear vigilant.
4. **Counter-authority:** What would an equally qualified authority on the other side say? If you can't find one, you haven't looked. Every expert consensus has dissenters.
5. **Symbols of authority:** Is the assessment swayed by symbols rather than substance? Credentials, titles, institutional affiliations, confident delivery. These correlate weakly with accuracy.
6. **Milgram reminder:** The most disturbing finding in social psychology: ordinary people will follow authority even when it violates their own moral judgment. Organisations amplify this.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume authority is substituting for evidence wherever an expert is cited without their reasoning being evaluated. The most dangerous authority is the one you don't think to question.
