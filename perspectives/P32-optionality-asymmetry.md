# Perspective: Optionality & Asymmetric Payoffs

**ID:** P32
**Domain:** Decision theory, finance, strategy, risk management
**Source:** Taleb (Antifragile, Black Swan), Munger, Kelly criterion, real options theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves decisions under uncertainty, investments, strategic choices, or any situation where outcomes are not binary.

## Lens

Optionality is the most underappreciated concept in decision-making. An option is the right — but not the obligation — to do something. Options with limited downside and unlimited upside are disproportionately valuable. Most assessments fail to identify, value, or structure for optionality.

1. **Option inventory:** What options exist? Real options (expand, contract, abandon, delay, switch), financial options, strategic options. Which are embedded in the current structure and which could be created?
2. **Asymmetry detection:** For each decision or investment: what is the distribution of outcomes? Is it symmetric or asymmetric? Is the upside capped while the downside is unlimited (negative optionality)? Or is the downside capped while upside is open (positive optionality)?
3. **Convexity:** Does the payoff curve bend upward (convex — gains accelerate, losses decelerate) or downward (concave — gains decelerate, losses accelerate)? Seek convexity. Avoid concavity.
4. **Kelly-type sizing:** For bets with positive expected value, how much should be risked? The Kelly criterion provides a mathematical answer: bet size should be proportional to edge divided by odds. Overbetting (beyond Kelly) guarantees ruin. Underbetting leaves value on the table. Is the assessment sizing appropriately?
5. **Cost of waiting:** Options have time value. What is gained by waiting? What is lost? In most complex decisions, waiting reveals information. The cost of delay must be weighed against the value of new information.
6. **Black swan exposure:** Is the assessment exposed to extreme, unpredictable events? Black swans can be positive or negative. The key question: is the exposure concave (vulnerable to negative extremes) or convex (benefits from positive extremes)?
7. **Barbell strategy:** In high-uncertainty environments, the optimal strategy is often a barbell — extremely safe on one side, extremely aggressive on the other, with nothing in the moderate middle. Would a barbell outperform the current approach?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment undervalues optionality. Assume embedded options exist that haven't been identified. Assume the structure has hidden negative convexity (limited upside, unlimited downside). Prefer structures with positive optionality — capped downside, open upside. The best investments are those where you can be wrong most of the time and still win when you're right.
