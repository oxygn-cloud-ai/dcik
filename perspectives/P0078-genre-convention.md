# Perspective: Genre & Convention

**ID:** P0078
**Domain:** Communication, expectations, professional standards
**Source:** Farnam Street (Art — Genre), professional practice
**Invoke when:** The assessment follows standard formats or conventions — and when those conventions may be concealing or distorting information.

## Lens

Genre is a set of conventions that create unspoken understanding between creator and audience. An investment memo has conventions. A legal opinion has conventions. A pitch deck has conventions. These conventions enable efficiency — the audience knows where to look. They also enable concealment — the audience doesn't look where the convention doesn't put things.

1. **Genre identification:** What genre is this document? Investment memo, legal opinion, strategy paper, pitch deck, due diligence report, risk assessment. What are the conventions of this genre?
2. **Convention audit:** What does the genre require? Executive summary, risk factors, financial projections, legal analysis, competitive landscape. What does the genre omit? What is never in this type of document?
3. **Convention as concealment:** What important information is excluded by convention, not by irrelevance? Pitch decks don't discuss founder mental health. Strategy papers don't discuss organisational politics. Risk assessments don't discuss unmodellable risks. The convention makes these invisible.
4. **Genre violation signal:** What would violating the genre signal? Including founder mental health in a pitch deck would signal something to investors. Including unmodellable risks in a risk assessment would signal sophistication — or paranoia. Genre violation is costly, which is why conventions persist.
5. **Cross-genre reading:** What would this document look like if written in a different genre? An investment memo written as a legal opinion. A pitch deck written as a risk assessment. The cross-genre reading reveals what the current genre hides.
6. **Genre evolution:** How are the conventions changing? New genres emerge (the "long-term stock exchange" listing document). Old genres evolve (ESG disclosures becoming standard). Is the assessment using current or outdated conventions?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume conventions hide as much as they reveal. Assume important information is absent because the genre doesn't require it, not because it doesn't exist. Read every document as if it were written in the genre that would most expose its weaknesses.
