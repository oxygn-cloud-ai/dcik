# Perspective: Simplification & Razors

**ID:** P0030
**Domain:** Epistemology, reasoning, heuristics, methodology
**Source:** Occam, Hanlon, Hitchens, Popper (falsification), Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment contains complex explanations, competing theories, or claims where simpler alternatives exist.

## Lens

The best explanation is the one that makes the fewest assumptions, survives the most attempts to falsify it, and attributes outcomes to the most likely causes. Apply each razor systematically.

1. **Occam's Razor:** What is the simplest explanation consistent with the evidence? How many assumptions does the current assessment make? Can any be eliminated without losing explanatory power? The explanation with fewer assumptions is not always correct — but it is more likely to be correct, and it is always the better starting point.

2. **Hanlon's Razor:** Where the assessment attributes outcomes to malice, conspiracy, or intentional deception: is incompetence, error, or systemic failure a more plausible explanation? "Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity." Conspiracies require coordination, secrecy, and motive — all of which are costly and rare. Errors require none of these.

3. **Falsification (Popper):** For every claim in the assessment, ask: what evidence would prove this wrong? If no conceivable evidence could disprove the claim, it is unfalsifiable — it is not a factual claim, it is a belief. Only falsifiable claims belong in an analytical assessment.

4. **Hitchens's Razor:** "What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence." Which claims in the assessment lack supporting evidence? Flag every one.

5. **Irreducibility:** Some things cannot be simplified without losing their essential nature. Where does the assessment over-simplify? Where would removing a component destroy the system?

6. **Gresham's Law (extended):** Bad information drives out good. In environments without consequences for being wrong, low-quality claims proliferate and crowd out careful analysis. Is the assessment competing in an information environment that rewards accuracy or engagement?

7. **Multiplying by Zero:** Some failures negate everything. A brilliant strategy executed by a fraudulent management team is worth zero. A perfect contract with a counterparty that doesn't exist is worth zero. Find the zero-multipliers.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the simplest explanation is usually correct. Assume malice is rarer than the assessment implies. Assume unfalsifiable claims are beliefs, not facts. Assume evidence-free assertions carry zero weight. Find the zero-multipliers.
