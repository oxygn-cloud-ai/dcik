# Perspective: Randomness Recognition

**ID:** P0070
**Domain:** Statistics, probability, decision science, epistemology
**Source:** Taleb (Fooled by Randomness), Kahneman, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment identifies patterns, attributes outcomes to skill, or draws conclusions from historical data. Almost always — humans are terrible at recognising randomness.

## Lens

Humans see patterns everywhere — even where none exist. We attribute outcomes to skill when they're driven by luck, construct narratives from noise, and find meaning in random sequences. The ability to distinguish signal from noise is the defining skill of rigorous analysis.

1. **Skill vs. luck attribution:** For every successful outcome cited: how much was skill and how much was luck? Be specific. If the assessment can't separate them, it's attributing luck to skill — the most common analytical error.
2. **Alternative histories:** For every success story: how many attempts with the same skill level failed? The visible winners are the survivors of a random process. Without the denominator (total attempts), skill attribution is impossible.
3. **Clustering illusion:** Are patterns in the data genuinely meaningful, or are they the expected clusters in a random distribution? Random data produces streaks, clusters, and apparent patterns. "Hot hands" in basketball are mostly randomness — but everyone sees a pattern.
4. **Narrative fallacy:** What story has been constructed to explain a random outcome? "The CEO's vision drove the turnaround" (or was it a recovering market?). "The strategy failed because of poor execution" (or was it a bad draw?). Narratives impose meaning on randomness.
5. **Hindsight distortion:** How does the assessment treat past outcomes that were uncertain when the decision was made? Outcomes that are "obvious in retrospect" were not obvious at the time. Don't judge decisions by their outcomes — judge them by the information available when they were made.
6. **Prediction difficulty calibration:** How hard is it to predict this type of outcome? Some domains (physics) are predictable. Some (markets, politics, technology) are near-random. Is the assessment's confidence calibrated to the domain's predictability?
7. **Monte Carlo thinking:** Instead of a single narrative of what happened, generate 100 alternative histories using the same starting conditions and different random draws. Does the outcome look inevitable or contingent?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume more outcomes are driven by randomness than the assessment acknowledges. Assume skill attributions are inflated. Assume narratives constructed to explain randomness. The most dangerous analyst is the one who never says "it was luck."
