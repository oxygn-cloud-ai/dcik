# Perspective: Path of Least Resistance

**ID:** P0063
**Domain:** Physics, psychology, organisational behaviour, design
**Source:** Farnam Street (Physics), behavioural economics, UX design
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes people will do the right thing, follow processes, or make optimal choices — rather than taking the easiest available path.

## Lens

Energy minimisation is a law of physics and a law of human behaviour. People, organisations, and systems gravitate toward the path of least resistance. The right choice, if harder than the wrong choice, will lose. Design must make the right choice the easy choice — or the easy choice will be what people do by default.

1. **Default path mapping:** For each decision the assessment assumes people will make: what is the easiest option? The one requiring the least effort, the fewest steps, the minimum cognitive load. That's what most people will do. Is it the option the assessment assumes?
2. **Friction audit:** Where does friction steer behaviour away from desired outcomes? Complex processes, unclear instructions, multiple approvals, confusing interfaces, hidden costs. Every friction point is a decision point where people choose the easier path.
3. **Friction as design:** Where is friction deliberately used to discourage unwanted behaviour? Making cancellation hard, making withdrawal costly, making complaints difficult. Is this ethical? Effective? Sustainable?
4. **Energy gradient:** What is the slope between options? A gentle slope (slightly easier to do the right thing) produces gradual adoption. A steep slope (overwhelmingly easier to do the wrong thing) produces rapid convergence on the wrong thing. How steep is the gradient?
5. **Organisational gravity:** Organisations have their own paths of least resistance: "we've always done it this way," "that's not my department," "it needs sign-off from legal." These are not malicious — they're energy-minimising. What organisational gravity is pulling behaviour away from the stated strategy?
6. **Redesign for ease:** If the desired behaviour were the easiest option, what would need to change? Remove steps, reduce cognitive load, pre-fill defaults, eliminate approvals, simplify choices. Redesign the path.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume people will take the easiest path. Assume friction points are steering behaviour in unintended directions. Assume the assessment's assumptions about human behaviour are optimistically wrong. Design the path so the right thing is the easy thing.
