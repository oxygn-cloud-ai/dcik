# Perspective: Compounding & Exponential Effects

**ID:** P28
**Domain:** Mathematics, growth dynamics, network effects, scale
**Source:** Munger, Buffett, systems theory, network economics
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves growth projections, market dynamics, competitive positioning, or any claim about "scaling."

## Lens

Compounding is the most powerful force in the universe — and the most misunderstood. Linear intuition fails catastrophically in exponential domains. Small differences in rate produce enormous differences in outcome over time.

1. **Is compounding operating?** Identify every feedback loop that could produce compounding: revenue reinvestment, learning curves, network effects, brand accumulation, data advantages, talent attraction.
2. **Rate sensitivity:** A 1% difference in compounding rate, sustained over 10 years, produces a ~10% difference in outcome. Over 30 years, it's ~35%. Over 50 years, it's ~65%. Are the relevant rates estimated with sufficient precision?
3. **Time horizon:** Compounding is invisible in year 1, noticeable in year 5, dominant in year 20. Does the assessment's time horizon capture the compounding zone?
4. **Exponential vs. linear assumptions:** Where does the assessment implicitly assume linear growth where compounding could produce exponential? Where does it assume exponential where linear is more likely?
5. **Network effects:** Does each additional user/customer/participant increase value for all others? If so, what type? Direct (Metcalfe), indirect (platform), data, or social?
6. **Power laws:** In domains with network effects or compounding advantages, outcomes follow power-law distributions. The #1 captures disproportionate share. Is the assessment modelling a normal distribution where a power law applies?
7. **Compounding's dark side:** Debt compounds. Technical debt compounds. Organisational dysfunction compounds. Environmental damage compounds. What negative forces are compounding in parallel with the positive ones?
8. **Diminishing returns:** Where does compounding hit diminishing returns? Market saturation? Talent scarcity? Regulatory friction? Resource constraints? Every exponential hits a limit eventually.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume linear projections in domains where compounding operates are wrong by orders of magnitude. Assume the compounding rate is lower than projected and the compounding period is shorter. Assume negative compounding forces exist and are being ignored. The difference between 7% and 8% compounding over 30 years is not 1% — it's a factor of 1.35x. Get the rates right.
