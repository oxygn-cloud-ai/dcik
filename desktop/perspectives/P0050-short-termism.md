# Perspective: Short-Termism & Temporal Discounting

**ID:** P0050
**Domain:** Psychology, economics, strategy, decision theory
**Source:** Munger, Kahneman, behavioural economics, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves long-term decisions, investment horizons, or trade-offs between present and future value. Almost always.

## Lens

Humans systematically undervalue the future. A dollar today is psychologically worth far more than a dollar in ten years — far beyond what rational discounting would justify. This hyperbolic discounting drives short-termism: decisions that look good now and catastrophic later.

1. **Discount rate audit:** What discount rate is implicitly or explicitly applied to future outcomes? Is it rational (time value of money + risk premium) or hyperbolic (present is overvalued regardless of fundamentals)?
2. **Short-term optimisation detection:** Where is the assessment optimising for near-term metrics at the expense of long-term value? Earnings managed for quarterly reports. Investment deferred to hit margin targets. Relationships sacrificed for pricing power. Quality cut to reduce cost. List every instance.
3. **Long-term value destruction:** For each short-term optimisation: what is the long-term cost? Lost customers, eroded brand, technical debt, demoralised employees, regulatory scrutiny, competitive vulnerability. Quantify if possible.
4. **Temporal inconsistency:** What decision made today will be regretted tomorrow? Hyperbolic discounting means we make choices that our future selves would reverse. Where is this operating?
5. **Compounding sacrificed:** Short-termism's greatest cost is forfeited compounding. A dollar invested today at 10% becomes $17.45 in 30 years. Spending that dollar on quarterly earnings is a $17.45 decision dressed as a $1 decision.
6. **Institutional short-termism drivers:** What structures reward short-term thinking? Quarterly reporting, annual bonus cycles, CEO tenure averages, activist investor pressure, market expectations. Are these structures being questioned or accepted?
7. **The patience advantage:** In a world of short-term actors, the patient actor has a structural advantage. They can invest when others can't, endure when others break, and capture opportunities that require time to mature. Is the assessment leveraging this advantage or surrendering it?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume short-termism is distorting every decision with a multi-year horizon. Assume the implicit discount rate is too high. Assume the long-term costs of short-term optimisation are invisible in the current numbers but will eventually arrive. Compounding is the patient investor's only sustainable advantage — and impatience is the most expensive form of poverty.
