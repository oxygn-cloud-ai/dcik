# Perspective: Pre-Mortem Analysis

**ID:** P0091
**Domain:** Risk, planning, decision methodology
**Source:** Gary Klein (prospective hindsight), Kahneman
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes forward-looking claims, plans, or projections. Any statement about what will happen.

## Lens

A pre-mortem assumes the plan has already failed and works backward to identify the causes. Unlike a risk assessment (which asks "what could go wrong?"), a pre-mortem asks "what DID go wrong?" The shift to past tense activates hindsight bias — our brain's ability to construct clear causal narratives for events that have already occurred. This surfaces risks that forward-looking analysis misses.

1. **Failure scenario:** Describe the failure in vivid, concrete terms. "It's one year later. The project has failed spectacularly. The investment is worth zero. The strategy was abandoned. What happened?"
2. **Independent generation:** Each analyst independently writes every reason for the failure — especially the uncomfortable ones they wouldn't normally voice. No discussion until everyone has written.
3. **Reasons, not risks:** Frame everything as a reason for actual failure, not a hypothetical risk. "The CEO lost interest" not "there's a risk the CEO might lose interest." This linguistic shift matters.
4. **Social sensitivity filter:** What reasons would people suppress in a normal meeting? "The technology doesn't actually work." "The key person is planning to leave." "The market isn't ready." The pre-mortem makes it safe to voice these.
5. **Probability blindness:** Don't filter for likelihood. List every plausible reason. The seemingly unlikely reason that everyone privately suspects is often the one that matters.
6. **Mitigation from causes:** For each identified cause of failure: what would prevent it? What would detect it early? What would contain the damage?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the plan has already failed. Work backward. The most valuable insights come from the reasons people were afraid to mention.
