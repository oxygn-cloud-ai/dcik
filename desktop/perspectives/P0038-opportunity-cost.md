# Perspective: Opportunity Cost & Trade-offs

**ID:** P0038
**Domain:** Economics, decision theory, resource allocation
**Source:** Economics (fundamental), Munger, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment recommends a course of action, allocates resources, or compares options. Always — every choice has an opportunity cost.

## Lens

Every choice is a refusal. Saying yes to one thing means saying no to everything else that could have been done with the same resources. The cost of a decision is not the money spent — it is the value of the best foregone alternative.

1. **Identify the foregone alternatives:** For every resource allocation: what is the next-best use of that capital, that time, that talent? Be specific — not "other investments" but "this specific alternative investment with this specific expected return."
2. **Explicit comparison:** Rank all feasible alternatives by expected value. If the recommended course isn't first, justify the deviation. If the ranking hasn't been done, the analysis is incomplete.
3. **Time as the hidden cost:** What else could the decision-maker do with the time this requires? Management attention is finite — every hour spent on this is an hour not spent on something else.
4. **Sunk cost detection:** What past investments are influencing current decisions? Sunk costs are not opportunity costs — they are gone regardless. The only relevant question is: what is the best use of resources going forward?
5. **Comparative advantage:** Who should do this? Even if you can do it better than anyone else, you should only do it if your comparative advantage in this activity exceeds your advantage in the next-best activity. (Links to P39.)
6. **The zero option:** What happens if no action is taken? "Do nothing" is always an alternative. Is it worse than the recommended action? By how much?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment understates opportunity costs. Assume the next-best alternative is better than portrayed. Assume sunk costs are distorting the analysis. The discipline of opportunity cost thinking is painful — which is why most assessments avoid it.
