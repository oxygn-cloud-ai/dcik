# Perspective: Scale Effects

**ID:** P45
**Domain:** Systems, strategy, organisational design, physics
**Source:** Geoffrey West (Scale), Farnam Street, Munger
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes claims about growth, scaling, or assumes that "more of the same" will work at different sizes.

## Lens

Things change as they grow. A startup of 10 people is not a smaller version of a company of 10,000 — it is a fundamentally different entity with different physics, different failure modes, and different management requirements. Scale is not linear.

1. **Sublinear vs. superlinear scaling:** Some things scale sublinearly (infrastructure costs per user decrease with scale — economies of scale). Some things scale superlinearly (coordination costs, bureaucracy, communication overhead increase faster than headcount). Which forces apply?
2. **Phase changes at scale:** At what size does the entity change qualitatively? 10→50→150 (Dunbar's number)→500→2,000→10,000. Each transition requires different management, communication, and culture.
3. **Diseconomies of scale:** What gets worse as the entity grows? Decision speed, innovation per employee, accountability clarity, cultural cohesion. Large entities optimise for predictability, not adaptability.
4. **Minimum viable scale:** What is the smallest size at which the entity can compete? Below minimum viable scale, the entity cannot cover fixed costs or compete for resources. Above it, scale advantages begin.
5. **Maximum efficient scale:** At what size do diseconomies overwhelm economies? Beyond this point, growth destroys value. Many businesses are pushed past their efficient scale by growth-obsessed management.
6. **Scaling what vs. scaling how:** The product scales (more users). Does the organisation scale (more employees)? Does the culture scale (more cohesion)? Does the quality scale (more consistency)? Each dimension has different scaling physics.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume diseconomies of scale are underappreciated. Assume the entity will hit scaling limits sooner than projected. Assume the management practices that work at current scale will fail at the next order of magnitude. Scaling is not doing more of the same — it is becoming a different thing.
