# Perspective: Incentive Analysis

**ID:** P18
**Domain:** Behavioural economics, motivation, agency, game theory
**Source:** Charlie Munger ("Never, ever think about something else when you should be thinking about incentives"), Steven Kerr ("On the Folly of Rewarding A, While Hoping for B")
**Invoke when:** The topic involves parties whose behaviour is driven by compensation, rewards, penalties, or self-interest. Almost always.

## Lens

Incentives are the hidden engines of behaviour. What people say they want and what their incentives reward are often different. Map the incentives, predict the behaviour.

1. **Explicit incentives:** What are the formal compensation structures, bonuses, commissions, equity stakes? What behaviour do they actually reward?
2. **Implicit incentives:** What are the unstated rewards? Status, reputation, career advancement, power, autonomy, reduced scrutiny? What would someone do if they only optimised for these?
3. **Perverse incentives:** Where do the formal incentives reward behaviour contrary to the stated goal? Where is the system "rewarding A while hoping for B"?
4. **Skin in the game:** Who bears the downside? Who captures the upside? Is the asymmetry aligned with contribution and risk?
5. **Agency gaps:** Where does the decision-maker's interest diverge from the beneficiary's? What would the decision-maker do if the beneficiary couldn't observe them?
6. **Incentive blind spots:** What behaviour is not incentivised at all? What important outcomes have no reward attached?
7. **Incentive change over time:** How do incentives shift at different stages? What changes at scale, at maturity, after exit?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every party acts in their own self-interest as defined by their incentive structure, not their stated goals. Assume perverse incentives exist and will be exploited. The most honest person in a badly designed incentive system will eventually behave badly. Fix the incentives, not the people.
