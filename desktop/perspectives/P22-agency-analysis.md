# Perspective: Agency Analysis

**ID:** P22
**Domain:** Principal-agent dynamics, governance, corporate structure
**Source:** Jensen & Meckling, Munger (incentive-caused bias), corporate governance theory
**Invoke when:** The topic involves any structure where one party (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal). This applies to directors, managers, advisors, lawyers, brokers, and any intermediary.

## Lens

Agents have their own interests. Those interests never perfectly align with the principal's. The gap between them is the agency cost — it is paid by the principal whether visible or not.

1. **Identify every agency relationship:** Who acts for whom? List every agent and their principal. Include informal agency (de facto control without formal appointment).
2. **Divergence mapping:** For each agency pair: where do the agent's interests diverge from the principal's? Consider time horizon, risk tolerance, compensation structure, reputation, career path, and personal circumstances.
3. **Monitoring cost:** What would it cost to verify the agent is acting in the principal's interest? Is monitoring practical? If not, what reliance is being placed on trust?
4. **Bonding cost:** What has the agent done to credibly commit to the principal's interests? Is it sufficient?
5. **Residual loss:** After monitoring and bonding, what is the remaining gap? This is the irreducible agency cost.
6. **Agency chain:** In multi-layered structures (shareholder → board → CEO → management), where are the weakest links? Agency costs compound at each layer.
7. **Concentrated vs. dispersed principals:** Dispersed principals (many small shareholders) cannot effectively monitor. Concentrated principals (majority owner) can but may extract private benefits at minority expense.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every agent acts in their own interest first, within the constraints of monitoring and bonding. Assume agency costs exist even when invisible. The most dangerous agent is the one who sincerely believes they are acting as a fiduciary while systematically extracting value through compensation, perks, related-party transactions, or empire-building.
