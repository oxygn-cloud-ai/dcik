# Perspective: Psychological & Cognitive Bias

**ID:** P0015
**Domain:** Cognitive biases, decision psychology, behavioural economics
**Invoke when:** Always. Human cognition is fallible. Every analysis is subject to bias. This perspective must be applied to the analysts themselves, not just the topic.

## Lens

1. **Confirmation bias:** Are we favouring evidence that supports our existing view? What evidence would change our mind? Have we sought it out?
2. **Overconfidence:** Are we too confident in our conclusions? What is the error bar? What is our track record on similar analyses?
3. **Anchoring:** Are we anchored to an initial number, position, or framing? What would we think if we started from a different anchor?
4. **Availability bias:** Are we overweighting recent, vivid, or easily-recalled examples? What are the base rates?
5. **Sunk cost:** Are we continuing because of past investment rather than future prospects? What would we do if starting fresh today?
6. **Framing effects:** How does the way the topic is presented affect our analysis? Would we reach different conclusions with different framing?
7. **Groupthink:** Are we converging on a view because others hold it? What would a genuinely independent analyst conclude?
8. **Narrative bias:** Are we constructing a coherent story that feels right but may be wrong? What are the holes in the narrative?
9. **Incentive-caused bias:** What do we (the analysts) gain from reaching particular conclusions? Are we truly disinterested?
10. **Complexity bias:** Are we adding complexity because it feels sophisticated? Would a simpler analysis be more robust?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume we are biased. Actively seek disconfirming evidence. State confidence levels explicitly. Distinguish between facts, inferences, and guesses. The most dangerous bias is the one we don't know we have.

## Required output

Applying this lens yields a concrete artifact, not just consideration:

1. **The single bias most likely distorting THIS assessment** (named), with the specific passage or claim where it operates.
2. **The debiasing test:** the concrete evidence or reframing that would show whether the bias is actually changing the conclusion, versus a false alarm.
3. **Severity** and **confidence** in the finding.

"No material bias detected" is valid only when the checks that were run are named.

## Falsifier

This lens's finding is wrong if the flagged claim is supported by evidence independent of the alleged bias, or if the reframing yields the same conclusion. Report which.
