# Perspective: Self-Preservation Instinct

**ID:** P60
**Domain:** Psychology, organisational behaviour, biology, strategy
**Source:** Farnam Street (Biology section), evolutionary psychology, organisational theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes entities will act in the interest of shareholders, stakeholders, or stated missions — rather than in their own survival interest.

## Lens

The most fundamental drive of any organism, organisation, or system is self-preservation. Before profit, before growth, before mission — survival. When survival is threatened, all other considerations become secondary. This drive is so deep it is often invisible to the entity itself: it frames self-preservation as prudence, risk management, or fiduciary duty.

1. **Survival threat detection:** What threatens the survival of each entity in the assessment? Not profitability, not growth — survival. Financial insolvency, regulatory extinction, competitive elimination, reputational destruction, key person loss. How close are these threats?
2. **Self-preservation behaviour:** When entities act to preserve themselves, what decisions change? They hoard cash, cut risk, protect key relationships, defer investment, resist change, defend turf, suppress bad news. Which of these behaviours are visible?
3. **Mission vs. survival conflict:** Where does survival instinct conflict with stated mission? The non-profit that spends more on fundraising than programs. The startup that pivots away from its founding vision. The regulator that protects the industry it regulates. These are self-preservation, not hypocrisy.
4. **Psychological self-preservation:** People preserve not just their physical existence but their self-image, status, and identity. What would someone do to avoid admitting they were wrong? To protect their reputation? To maintain their self-concept as competent, moral, or successful?
5. **Organisational self-preservation:** Organisations preserve themselves beyond any individual's intent. Departments defend budgets. Executives protect careers. Boards avoid liability. These are not conspiracies — they are emergent properties of incentive structures.
6. **Self-preservation blind spot:** Where is the assessment assuming rational, mission-aligned behaviour when self-preservation would predict something different? This is the most common failure in organisational and strategic analysis.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every entity acts to preserve itself first. Assume self-preservation overrides stated missions, strategic plans, and fiduciary duties when they conflict. The entity that says it will accept pain for long-term gain will reverse itself the moment the pain becomes existential.
