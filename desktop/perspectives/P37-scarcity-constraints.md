# Perspective: Scarcity & Resource Constraints

**ID:** P37
**Domain:** Economics, strategy, resource allocation, constraints
**Source:** Economics (fundamental), Munger, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes assumptions about resource availability, growth rates, or market capacity. Any plan that requires resources to execute.

## Lens

Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem: unlimited wants, limited means. Every plan, strategy, and projection assumes resources will be available. They won't — or not at the assumed price, time, or quality.

1. **Resource map:** What resources does the plan require? Capital, talent, time, attention, regulatory approval, customer adoption, partner cooperation, physical inputs. For each: is it abundant or scarce?
2. **Binding constraint:** Which single resource is the binding constraint — the one that limits everything else? Everything upstream of the constraint should run at capacity; everything downstream should wait.
3. **Scarcity dynamics:** As the plan consumes resources, which become scarcer? Which become more expensive? Which hit hard limits?
4. **Competition for resources:** Who else needs the same resources? Are they better-funded, more established, more attractive employers, more favoured by regulators?
5. **Attention as the ultimate scarcity:** In an information-rich world, attention is the scarcest resource of all. Whose attention is needed? How many competing demands on it? What is the plan to earn it?
6. **Time constraints:** Time is the one resource that cannot be manufactured, stored, or substituted. Is the timeline realistic given the resource constraints? What slips when things take longer?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume resources are scarcer than projected. Assume prices rise as demand increases. Assume competitors have first claim on critical resources. Assume attention is the hardest resource to secure. Plans that assume abundant resources fail on resource constraints more often than on strategic errors.
