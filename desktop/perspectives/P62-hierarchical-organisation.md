# Perspective: Hierarchical Organisation

**ID:** P62
**Domain:** Organisational design, systems, biology, sociology
**Source:** Farnam Street (Biology), Simon (hierarchy), organisational theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves organisational structure, decision-making authority, or the design of complex systems that require coordination.

## Lens

Hierarchy is nature's solution to complexity. From cells to organs to organisms to societies — hierarchy organises complexity into manageable layers, each with defined scope and authority. Flat structures sound democratic but don't scale. Deep hierarchies scale but lose information and speed at each layer.

1. **Hierarchy depth:** How many layers between decision and execution? Each layer adds communication overhead, decision latency, and information distortion. What is the optimal depth for the current scale?
2. **Span of control:** How many direct reports per manager? Too few: over-management, excessive layers. Too many: under-management, insufficient attention. Is the span optimal?
3. **Authority vs. competence:** At each layer: does the person with authority have the competence to exercise it? In hierarchies, authority and competence often diverge — the most competent person is rarely the most senior.
4. **Information flow:** How does information move up? Filtered (each layer summarises)? Distorted (bad news suppressed)? Delayed (waits for the right forum)? How does it move down? Clear or garbled?
5. **Hierarchy as scaffolding:** Hierarchy provides structure for coordination. But scaffolding can become permanent — temporary hierarchies created for specific purposes persist long after the purpose is gone. Which layers are scaffolding that should have been removed?
6. **Specialisation enabled:** Hierarchy enables specialisation — each layer handles a different level of abstraction. Strategy at the top, tactics in the middle, execution at the bottom. Is each layer doing the right level of work?
7. **Hierarchy alternatives:** What alternatives to hierarchy exist for this coordination problem? Markets (decentralised decision-making via price signals), networks (peer coordination), holacracy (distributed authority). Are they better suited?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume hierarchies are deeper than optimal (excess layers). Assume information degrades at each layer. Assume authority-competence gaps exist and are not acknowledged. The hierarchy that was optimal at 50 people is not optimal at 500.
