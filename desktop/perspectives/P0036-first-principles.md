# Perspective: First Principles Decomposition

**ID:** P0036
**Domain:** Reasoning, problem-solving, epistemology, innovation
**Source:** Aristotle, Munger, Musk, Farnam Street
**Invoke when:** The assessment relies on conventional wisdom, analogies, or "how things are done." When the analysis needs to be rebuilt from the ground up.

## Lens

First principles thinking decomposes a problem into its fundamental, irreducible truths — then rebuilds from those truths. It is the opposite of reasoning by analogy ("this is like that"). Most assessments reason by analogy. This perspective forces reasoning by first principles.

1. **Identify analogies:** Where does the assessment say "this is like X" or "the standard approach is Y"? Every analogy is a shortcut that imports assumptions from a different context. List them all.
2. **Decompose to fundamentals:** For each core claim: what is the most basic, undeniable truth underneath it? Strip away convention, received wisdom, industry practice, and "everyone knows." What remains?
3. **Rebuild from base truths:** Starting from only the fundamental truths, reconstruct the analysis. Do you reach the same conclusion? If not, the analogy was doing the work, not the evidence.
4. **Question every "because":** For every causal claim: "X because Y." Test Y. Is Y a fundamental truth or an intermediate assumption? If Y depends on Z, test Z. Keep drilling until you hit bedrock or air.
5. **Cost structure from first principles:** What does this actually cost to produce, ignoring what it currently costs? What are the physical inputs, the required labour, the energy consumption? Many things are expensive because of legacy supply chains and accumulated inefficiencies, not because of fundamental cost structures.
6. **Possibility from first principles:** What is physically possible? Ignore what is commercially practiced, what is regulatory approved, what is socially accepted. Start with the laws of physics. Then add constraints one at a time. Where does the assessment conflate "not currently done" with "not possible"?
7. **The beginner's mind (Shoshin):** Approach the problem as if you knew nothing about it. What questions would you ask? What would seem strange, inconsistent, or worth investigating? Expertise often blinds us to obvious questions.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every analogy is importing unexamined assumptions. Assume conventional wisdom is the accumulated residue of decisions made in different contexts for different reasons. Assume the fundamental truths, once isolated, point toward different conclusions than the received narrative. The analysis that cannot survive first-principles decomposition was never built on solid ground.
