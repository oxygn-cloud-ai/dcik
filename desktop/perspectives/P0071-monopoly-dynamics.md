# Perspective: Monopoly Dynamics

**ID:** P71
**Domain:** Economics, competition, regulation, strategy
**Source:** Economics, Munger, Farnam Street, antitrust theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves market structure, competitive dynamics, or any entity with significant market power.

## Lens

Monopoly and competition are opposites that feed each other. Competition drives innovation and lower prices. Monopoly funds moonshots and captures returns that justify investment. Every company wants to be a monopoly and wants its suppliers and customers to be perfectly competitive. The dynamics between these poles shape industries.

1. **Monopoly degree:** Where on the spectrum from perfect competition to pure monopoly does the entity sit? What is its market share? Pricing power? Barriers to entry? Substitutability of its product?
2. **Monopoly source:** What creates the monopoly power? Natural monopoly (infrastructure, network effects), legal monopoly (patents, licences, regulation), strategic monopoly (brand, scale, data), coercive monopoly (government-granted exclusivity). Different sources have different durability.
3. **Monopoly behaviour:** How does the entity exercise its power? Price above competitive levels, restrict output, reduce quality, slow innovation, acquire threats, lobby for regulatory barriers. Which behaviours are visible?
4. **Monopoly cost to society:** What is the deadweight loss? Higher prices, reduced output, suppressed innovation, captured regulators, concentrated wealth. Quantify the cost.
5. **Monopoly defence:** How does the monopoly defend itself? Regulatory capture, patent thickets, exclusive contracts, predatory pricing against entrants, acquisition of startups before they become threats, litigation. Which defences are active?
6. **Monopoly fragility:** Monopolies appear invulnerable until they're not. What could break this one? Technological disruption, regulatory action, market entry, consumer revolt, internal decay. Monopolies often die from within — complacency, bureaucracy, innovation starvation.
7. **Competition as counterforce:** Where is competition emerging? Adjacent markets, substitute products, alternative business models, regulatory-mandated competition. Competition doesn't have to beat the monopoly head-on — it can make it irrelevant.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume monopoly power is being exercised even when it appears benign. Assume monopolies underinvest in innovation and overinvest in defence. Assume competition is emerging from directions the monopoly isn't watching. The most dangerous monopolist is the one that believes its own narrative about serving customers.
