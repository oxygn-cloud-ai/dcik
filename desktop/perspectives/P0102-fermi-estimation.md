# Perspective: Fermi Estimation

**ID:** P102
**Domain:** Quantitative reasoning, estimation, modelling
**Source:** Enrico Fermi, cc-thinking-skills
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes quantitative claims without supporting data — or when orders of magnitude matter more than precision.

## Lens

Fermi estimation decomposes a seemingly impossible-to-estimate quantity into components that can be individually estimated, then combines them. It produces order-of-magnitude accuracy from minimal data. The skill is finding the right decomposition and calibrating each component.

1. **Decomposition:** Break the quantity into estimable components. "How many piano tuners in Chicago?" → pianos in Chicago ÷ pianos per tuner per year. Each sub-question is easier than the whole.
2. **Component estimation:** For each component: what is the plausible range? What is the most likely value? Use benchmarks, analogies, and common knowledge. Express uncertainty explicitly.
3. **Error propagation:** How do errors in individual components combine? Independent errors tend to cancel (some high, some low). Correlated errors compound.
4. **Order-of-magnitude sufficiency:** Is order-of-magnitude accuracy sufficient for the decision? If the answer is $10M and the decision threshold is $50M, precision to the nearest $100k is unnecessary. Don't over-model.
5. **Fermi as bullshit detection:** When a claim seems implausible, Fermi it. "This market is $100B." Decompose → estimate components → does it reach $100B? Fermi estimates expose inflated claims.
6. **Calibration improvement:** Track Fermi estimates against outcomes. Which components were systematically overestimated or underestimated? Improve calibration over time.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume quantitative claims without supporting decomposition are inflated. Fermi the numbers. If the components don't add up to the claim, the claim is wrong.
