# Perspective: Algorithms vs. Judgment

**ID:** P79
**Domain:** Decision science, AI, methodology, organisational design
**Source:** Farnam Street (Algorithms), Kahneman, Meehl (clinical vs. statistical prediction)
**Invoke when:** The assessment relies on human judgment where algorithmic or systematic approaches might outperform, or vice versa.

## Lens

In most domains, simple algorithms outperform expert judgment. Not because algorithms are brilliant — because humans are inconsistent. We weigh the same evidence differently depending on mood, fatigue, recency, and framing. The algorithm applies the same weights every time. The question is not whether judgment or algorithms are better in the abstract — it's which is better in this specific domain, with this specific decision.

1. **Judgment vs. algorithm domain:** Is this a domain where algorithms outperform judgment? Prediction (clinical vs. statistical), hiring (structured interviews vs. unstructured), investing (rules-based vs. discretionary), diagnosis (checklist vs. intuition). Most domains favour algorithms — is the assessment aware of this?
2. **Algorithm opportunity:** Where could a simple rule replace expensive judgment? "If X, then Y" — no committee, no deliberation, no delay. What decisions are being made by judgment that could be algorithmised, freeing judgment for genuinely novel situations?
3. **Algorithm limitation:** Where would algorithmic decision-making fail? Novel situations with no training data, domains where the rules are changing, decisions requiring empathy or moral reasoning, situations where the algorithm's training data encodes historical bias. What can't be algorithmised?
4. **Algorithm transparency:** If an algorithm is making or informing decisions, can its reasoning be explained? "The algorithm said so" is not an explanation. Black-box algorithms in high-stakes decisions create accountability gaps.
5. **Judgment consistency:** How consistent is the judgment being applied? Would the same person make the same decision on a different day? Would different people make the same decision? Inconsistency is the hidden cost of judgment-based decision-making.
6. **Hybrid approach:** Where should algorithms inform judgment rather than replace it? The algorithm flags anomalies; the human investigates. The algorithm scores candidates; the human interviews. The algorithm identifies risks; the human decides. Hybrid approaches capture the best of both.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume judgment-based decisions are less consistent and less accurate than their practitioners believe. Assume simple algorithms would outperform expert judgment in most domains in the assessment. The resistance to algorithms is rarely about accuracy — it's about the discomfort of being replaced by a rule.
