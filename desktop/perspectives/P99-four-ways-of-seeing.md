# Perspective: Four Ways of Seeing

**ID:** P99
**Domain:** Intelligence analysis, perspective-taking, empathy, strategy
**Source:** U.S. Army Red Team Handbook, UFMCS
**Invoke when:** The assessment views a situation from only one perspective — typically the subject's own.

## Lens

Four Ways of Seeing examines a situation from four distinct perspectives: (1) how you see yourself, (2) how the adversary sees itself, (3) how you see the adversary, and (4) how the adversary sees you. The gaps between these perspectives reveal blind spots, misunderstandings, and opportunities.

1. **Self-perception (How I see myself/us):** What is the subject's self-image? Strengths, values, intentions, constraints. This is usually the default perspective of the assessment.
2. **Adversary self-perception (How they see themselves):** What is the counterparty's self-image? Their strengths, values, intentions, constraints from their own perspective. This requires genuine effort to see the world through their eyes.
3. **Our view of them (How I see the adversary):** How does the subject perceive the counterparty? This often conflates how they actually are with how we imagine them to be.
4. **Their view of us (How the adversary sees me):** How does the counterparty perceive the subject? This is the most neglected perspective — and often the most revealing. The adversary's actions are driven by their perception of you, not by your perception of yourself.
5. **Gap analysis:** Where are the divergences? They see themselves differently from how you see them. They see you differently from how you see yourself. The gaps are where surprises originate.
6. **Expansion:** Beyond "adversary" to any relevant actor: partners, regulators, customers, employees, the public. Multiple four-way views for multiple actors.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment is written entirely from perspective 1. Assume the adversary's view of the subject is radically different from the subject's self-view. The most important perspective is the one the assessment never considered.
