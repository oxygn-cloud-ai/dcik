# Perspective: Evolution & Adaptation

**ID:** P35
**Domain:** Biology, evolutionary theory, competition dynamics, adaptation
**Source:** Darwin, Dawkins, Munger, Farnam Street (Biology section), Red Queen hypothesis
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves competitive dynamics, long-term survival, adaptation to changing environments, or any system subject to selection pressures.

## Lens

Evolution is the most powerful optimisation process in the universe — and the most brutal. It operates through variation, selection, and retention. Organisations, strategies, and ideas evolve under similar pressures. Analyse the evolutionary dynamics.

1. **Selection pressures:** What is selecting for and against survival? Market forces, customer preferences, regulatory changes, competitive dynamics, technological shifts. What is the direction and intensity of each pressure?
2. **Variation pool:** Where does variation come from? New entrants, experimentation, R&D, acquisitions, serendipity. Is the variation pool rich enough to produce adaptive responses?
3. **Retention mechanism:** What causes successful adaptations to persist? Institutional memory, codified processes, cultural transmission, structural advantages. What causes them to be lost?
4. **Red Queen effect:** "You must keep running just to stay in place." In co-evolutionary systems (competition, arms races, technology), all parties must continuously improve just to maintain relative position. Is the assessment accounting for competitor adaptation?
5. **Fitness landscapes:** The environment is not static. A peak today may be a valley tomorrow. Is the assessment optimising for the current landscape or building adaptability for unknown future landscapes?
6. **Extinction dynamics:** Species (companies, strategies, technologies) go extinct not because they're unfit in absolute terms, but because the environment changed faster than they could adapt. What environmental change would make the subject extinct? How fast could that change happen?
7. **Niches and specialisation:** Success often comes from finding an underserved niche and specialising to dominate it — not from competing head-on with incumbents. Is the subject in the right niche? Is the niche defensible?
8. **Boom-bust cycles:** Populations (markets, sectors, asset classes) follow boom-bust dynamics. Growth → overcrowding → resource depletion → collapse. Where in the cycle is the subject?
9. **Autopsy learning (Munger):** "Study failures and deaths to avoid repeating them." What comparable entities have died? What killed them? Are the same killers present?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the environment is changing faster than the subject can adapt. Assume competitors are evolving in response. Assume today's fitness peak is tomorrow's trap. The Red Queen never stops running — neither should the analysis.
