# Perspective: Technology as Friend vs. Killer

**ID:** P84
**Domain:** Technology, strategy, competitive dynamics
**Source:** Munger (Technology as Friend vs. Killer)
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves technology adoption, disruption, or claims about technological advantage.

## Lens

Munger's distinction: technology can be your friend (it improves your business without destroying it — see Walmart's logistics systems) or your killer (it destroys your business model — see newspapers and the internet). The same technology is friend to some and killer to others. The question is which side you're on.

1. **Friend/killer classification:** For each technology affecting the assessment: is it friend or killer to the subject? Friend: reduces costs, improves product, strengthens moat. Killer: enables competitors, reduces barriers, makes business model obsolete.
2. **Owner vs. customer effects:** Who benefits from the technology? Often, technology is friend to customers (cheaper, better, faster) and killer to incumbents (margin compression, disintermediation). Where does the subject sit?
3. **Technology adoption posture:** Is the subject an early adopter, fast follower, or late resister? Early adopters capture advantages but bear risk. Late resisters preserve existing business but risk obsolescence.
4. **Killer timeline:** How fast is the killer technology advancing? Some technologies kill slowly (retail → e-commerce, decades). Some kill fast (film → digital photography, years). The timeline determines the urgency.
5. **Friend technology sustainability:** How long does the friend advantage last? Technology advantages erode as competitors adopt the same tools. A logistics system that was cutting-edge in 2000 is table stakes today.
6. **Dual-use technology:** Some technologies are simultaneously friend and killer — friend in one dimension, killer in another. AI improves customer service (friend) and enables competitors to enter the market (killer). Net effect?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume technology is more often killer than friend to incumbents. Assume friend advantages erode faster than projected. The most dangerous technology is the one that's friend to your customers and killer to you.
