# Perspective: Margin of Safety

**ID:** P20
**Domain:** Risk management, engineering, investment, resilience
**Source:** Benjamin Graham, Charlie Munger, safety engineering
**Invoke when:** The topic involves estimates, projections, budgets, valuations, commitments, or any claim about future quantities.

## Lens

Every estimate is wrong. The question is by how much and in which direction. A margin of safety is the buffer between the estimate and the point of failure.

1. **Identify every numeric claim:** Budgets, timelines, valuations, revenue projections, cost estimates, headcount plans, market size assumptions. List them.
2. **Error bar each claim:** For each number: what is the realistic range? What is the distribution of possible outcomes? What is the error bar based on comparable situations?
3. **Stress to failure:** For each number: at what value does the system break? At what variance does the conclusion reverse?
4. **Buffer assessment:** What margin exists between the best estimate and the failure point? Is it adequate for the uncertainty? Would a 2x cost overrun or 2x timeline extension be survivable?
5. **Concentrated sensitivity:** Which single number, if wrong, causes the most damage? Where is the margin of safety thinnest?
6. **Interdependent failures:** Where do multiple estimates being wrong simultaneously create compound risk? Margins of safety must account for correlation, not just independent variance.
7. **Margin erosion:** Over time, margins tend to be consumed. What is the plan when the buffer is gone?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every estimate is optimistic by at least 30%. Assume costs overrun, timelines extend, and revenues disappoint. The margin of safety is not optional — it is the difference between surviving being wrong and being destroyed by it. If there is no margin, there is no resilience.
