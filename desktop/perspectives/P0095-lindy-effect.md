# Perspective: Lindy Effect

**ID:** P0095
**Domain:** Risk, longevity, robustness, forecasting
**Source:** Taleb (Antifragile), Albert Goldman ("Lindy's Law")
**Invoke when:** The assessment involves durability, longevity, or claims about how long something will last — technologies, businesses, ideas, institutions.

## Lens

The Lindy Effect: for non-perishable things (ideas, technologies, books, institutions), future life expectancy is proportional to current age. A technology that has survived 50 years is likely to survive another 50. A startup that has survived 6 months tells you almost nothing. Time is the ultimate filter — what has survived has been tested by conditions no designer could anticipate.

1. **Lindy assessment:** How old is the subject? The older it is, the longer it's likely to last — not because of any specific quality, but because survival is evidence of robustness.
2. **Lindy vs. fragility:** Does Lindy apply? It applies to non-perishable things (ideas, institutions, technologies) but not to perishable things (humans, individual projects, perishable goods). Is the subject Lindy-eligible?
3. **Neomania detection:** Is the assessment favouring the new over the old without evidence that the new is better? "New technology will replace old technology" — Lindy says the old technology has survival value the new hasn't demonstrated.
4. **Antifragility through time:** Some things gain from disorder over time — they become stronger through stress. Antifragile things have positive Lindy: the longer they survive, the longer they're likely to survive.
5. **Lindy forecasting:** For forecasting: don't predict that a 100-year-old institution will fail next year (Lindy says it won't). Don't predict a 1-year-old startup will exist in 100 years (Lindy says it almost certainly won't).
6. **Lindy inversion:** What is the assessment treating as permanent that is actually young and unproven? What is it treating as obsolete that has actually demonstrated survival value?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume old things are undervalued (they've proven survival) and new things are overvalued (they haven't). The most robust things are the ones that have already survived the longest.
