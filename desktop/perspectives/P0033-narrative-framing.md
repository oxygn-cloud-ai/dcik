# Perspective: Narrative & Framing

**ID:** P33
**Domain:** Communication, psychology, rhetoric, storytelling
**Source:** Farnam Street (Art/Narrative section), Kahneman (framing effects), Cialdini, narrative economics
**Invoke when:** The assessment tells a story — about a company, an investment, a strategy, a market. Always. Every assessment has a narrative.

## Lens

Humans think in stories, not statistics. The most persuasive assessment is the one with the best narrative — not the one with the best evidence. This perspective examines the narrative itself: what story is being told, who benefits from it, and what evidence it obscures.

1. **Identify the narrative:** What is the story the assessment tells? "Plucky startup disrupts stagnant industry." "Visionary founder sees what others missed." "Inevitable trend reaches tipping point." State the narrative explicitly.
2. **Narrative fit vs. evidence fit:** Is evidence being selected to fit the narrative, or is the narrative being built from the evidence? The difference is everything.
3. **Framing effects:** How does the presentation frame the analysis? What is emphasised? What is buried? What is the first thing the reader sees? The first thing sets the anchor.
4. **Character assignment:** Who is the hero? The villain? The victim? The supporting cast? Character assignments simplify complex realities — real people don't fit into narrative roles.
5. **Plot structure:** What is the dramatic arc? Struggle → triumph? Descent → redemption? Inevitable rise? Plot structures impose meaning on events that may have none.
6. **Setting manipulation:** How does the context (market, era, competitive landscape) get portrayed? Is it "the perfect storm" or "the ideal environment"? Settings can be framed to make any strategy look brilliant or doomed.
7. **Contrast and comparison:** What is the assessment compared to? The choice of comparison determines the conclusion. Compared to the worst-case, everything looks good. Compared to the best-case, nothing does.
8. **Audience awareness:** Who is this written for? What do they want to hear? What evidence would they find most persuasive regardless of its quality? The audience shapes the narrative.
9. **Counter-narrative:** What is the opposite story? Write it. Is it less plausible — or just less convenient?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the assessment's narrative is driving its conclusions, not the other way around. Assume evidence that contradicts the story has been minimised or omitted. The most dangerous assessment is the one where the story is so compelling that no one checks the numbers.
