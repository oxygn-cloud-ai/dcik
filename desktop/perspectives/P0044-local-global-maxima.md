# Perspective: Local & Global Maxima

**ID:** P0044
**Domain:** Optimisation, strategy, decision theory, mathematics
**Source:** Mathematics, Farnam Street, Munger, strategy literature
**Invoke when:** The assessment recommends optimisation, improvement, or "better" versions of the current approach. Any claim that the current path is optimal.

## Lens

A local maximum is the highest point in the immediate neighbourhood — but not the highest point overall. To reach the global maximum, you often have to go downhill first. Most optimisation traps the optimiser at a local peak.

1. **Current peak check:** Is the current strategy optimising for a local maximum? What would it look like to step back — accept worse short-term outcomes — to reach a higher long-term peak?
2. **Downhill to climb higher:** What would need to get worse before it could get better? What current advantages, relationships, or revenue streams would need to be sacrificed?
3. **Optimisation addiction:** Continuous incremental improvement feels productive. It is productive — at climbing the current hill. Is the current hill the right hill?
4. **Neighbouring peaks:** What other peaks exist in the landscape? Different business models, strategies, markets, approaches. Are any higher? How would you know without exploring?
5. **Gradient vs. landscape:** Following the gradient (local optimisation) always leads to a peak — but which one depends entirely on the starting point. Where did the current strategy start, and how has that starting point shaped where it ended up?
6. **Randomised restart:** In complex optimisation problems, sometimes the best strategy is to start over from a random point. When was the last time the strategy was rebuilt from scratch? (Links to P36.)

## Default adversarial stance

Assume the current strategy is at a local maximum. Assume the effort required to climb higher involves first going downhill, and that this is why it hasn't been done. The comfort of incremental improvement masks the trap of local optimisation.
