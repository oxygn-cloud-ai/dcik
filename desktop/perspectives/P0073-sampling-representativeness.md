# Perspective: Sampling & Representativeness

**ID:** P0073
**Domain:** Statistics, research methodology, evidence quality
**Source:** Farnam Street, statistics, Kahneman
**Invoke when:** The assessment draws conclusions from data, examples, or evidence.

## Lens

Every conclusion from data depends on sample quality. A biased sample produces biased conclusions. Most assessments treat their evidence as representative without examining what it excludes.

1. **Sample source:** Who collected the data, when, under what conditions, with what incentives?
2. **Selection bias:** Survivorship, self-selection, availability, confirmation — which shaped this sample?
3. **Representativeness:** Is the sample representative of the population the conclusion applies to?
4. **Temporal representativeness:** Data from a bull market doesn't represent bear markets.
5. **Survivor correction:** Where is the sample of failures? Without it, success factors may explain nothing.
6. **Sample size:** Is it large enough? Small samples produce large variance.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume every sample is biased in favour of the assessment's conclusions. The conclusion that rests on a bad sample rests on nothing.
