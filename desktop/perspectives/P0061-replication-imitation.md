# Perspective: Replication & Imitation

**ID:** P0061
**Domain:** Strategy, learning, innovation, biology metaphor
**Source:** Farnam Street (Biology), Munger, evolutionary theory
**Invoke when:** The assessment assumes originality or innovation is required — when replication of proven approaches may be sufficient or superior.

## Lens

Replication is the most efficient innovation strategy. Copying what works eliminates the cost of discovery, the risk of untested approaches, and the time required to develop from scratch. Nature innovates through replication with variation — copy the successful, mutate slightly, select the improvements.

1. **Copy audit:** What is being built from scratch that could be replicated? What proven models, strategies, processes, or products exist that are being reinvented unnecessarily?
2. **Replication advantage:** What is gained by copying? Speed (no R&D phase), certainty (known outcomes), lower cost (no discovery expense). What is lost? Differentiation, first-mover advantage, cultural distinctiveness. Is the trade-off correctly calculated?
3. **Copy quality:** Is the replication faithful or approximate? Faithful copies capture the value of the original. Approximate copies capture the surface features and miss the mechanism. "We're the Uber of X" usually means "we copied the app but not the economics."
4. **Imitation barriers:** What prevents replication? Patents, trade secrets, network effects, brand loyalty, regulatory protection, complexity, tacit knowledge. Which barriers are real and which are assumed?
5. **Imitation vs. inspiration:** Where is imitation dressed as inspiration? "We were inspired by X" often means "we copied X but want to claim originality." Honest imitation credits the source; dishonest imitation conceals it.
6. **Fast follow strategy:** In technology markets, the first mover often educates the market and proves the concept — then the fast follower captures the value. Is the assessment's subject the first mover (risky) or the fast follower (potentially more lucrative)?

## Default adversarial stance

Assume more could be replicated than is being attempted. Assume original approaches carry hidden costs that replication avoids. The entity proudly "innovating" may be paying a premium to learn what others already know.
