# Perspective: Socratic Elenchus

**ID:** P0093
**Domain:** Philosophy, epistemology, critical reasoning
**Source:** Socrates/Plato, analytic philosophy
**Invoke when:** The assessment makes claims to knowledge or relies on definitions that may not survive cross-examination.

## Lens

The Socratic elenchus (cross-examination) tests whether a claim to knowledge is internally consistent. The questioner need not know the truth — they only need to show that the respondent's own beliefs contradict each other. This surfaces hidden assumptions and forces either revision or humility.

1. **Thesis extraction:** State the core claim as a clear, falsifiable thesis. "X is true." "Y is the right course of action." "Z is the cause." If it can't be stated clearly, it can't be tested.
2. **Commitment elicitation:** What other beliefs does the assessment commit to, implicitly or explicitly? These are the premises that, if inconsistent with the thesis, would refute it.
3. **Contradiction surfacing:** Do any of the assessment's own statements contradict each other? "We're the market leader" + "We have 3% market share." "The risk is low" + "We've never done this before." Contradictions often hide in different sections of a long document.
4. **Definition testing:** How are key terms defined? "Success." "Risk." "Value." "Fair." Socratic testing often reveals that the respondent doesn't actually know what they mean — the definition collapses under examination.
5. **Aporia acceptance:** The Socratic method often ends in aporia — acknowledged ignorance. This is not failure. Recognising that you don't know is epistemically superior to confidently believing something false.
6. **Meno's paradox awareness:** How can you seek what you don't know? The Socratic method doesn't generate novel knowledge from nothing — it clarifies and tests existing beliefs. Its power is negative: it eliminates false certainty.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume key terms are undefined or inconsistently defined. Assume hidden contradictions exist between different sections. Cross-examine the assessment as Socrates would — not to humiliate, but to test whether it actually knows what it claims to know.
