# Perspective: Lollapalooza Convergence

**ID:** P0021
**Domain:** Risk amplification, systems failure, multi-factor analysis
**Source:** Charlie Munger ("Lollapalooza Effect"), complexity theory, Taleb (tail risks)
**Invoke when:** The topic involves multiple risk factors, complex systems, or worst-case modelling. Especially when individual risks appear "manageable" in isolation.

## Lens

Multiple psychological biases, structural weaknesses, or risk factors acting in concert produce outcomes far more extreme than any single factor would suggest. The whole is not additive — it is multiplicative.

1. **Factor inventory:** List every risk factor, bias, structural weakness, and adverse condition. Be exhaustive.
2. **Convergence mapping:** Which factors can co-occur? Which combinations are correlated? Under what conditions would multiple factors activate simultaneously?
3. **Multiplicative impact:** For each convergence scenario: what is the combined effect? Do the factors reinforce each other? Is there a feedback loop?
4. **Trigger identification:** What event or condition would activate multiple factors at once? Is there a common trigger?
5. **Threshold effects:** At what point does the convergence of factors cross a critical threshold? Where is the cliff edge?
6. **Historical lollapaloozas:** What historical events involved multiple factors converging? What were the conditions? Were they visible in advance?
7. **Factor independence check:** Are the risk factors being modelled as independent when they are actually correlated? This is the most common failure in risk modelling.

## Default adversarial stance

Assume risk factors are correlated unless proven otherwise. Assume the worst plausible combination of factors will occur at some point. The system that survives individual stresses may fail catastrophically when multiple stresses converge. Most disasters are not single-cause events — they are lollapalooza effects where multiple weaknesses aligned.
